# A 12–18 Month Deep Mastery Curriculum for Event-Driven Architecture and Adjacent Distributed Systems

## TL;DR

This curriculum assumes you have already completed a one-month foundation covering Fowler's EDA patterns, Kafka fundamentals, the transactional outbox with Debezium, event sourcing and CQRS on a wallet domain, Kafka Streams for fraud detection, and the canonical case studies from LinkedIn, Uber, Netflix, Airbnb, Shopify, LINE, Coupang, and Kakao. From here we go vertical, not horizontal. Over the next 12–18 months you will read the foundational distributed-systems literature in its original form, internalize the streaming-systems model end-to-end, read enough Kafka source code to talk to its committers as peers, build a streaming engine, a log broker, and an HFT-style matching engine from scratch, and write TLA+ specifications for protocols you actually deploy. The plan is opinionated, sequenced, and aimed squarely at producing an architect who can be hired by Confluent, LinkedIn's Kafka team, or a top-tier quant shop, and who can also lead EDA adoption end-to-end at your current employer.

The single most important shift in the next phase is moving from "I can use Kafka" to "I can derive Kafka from first principles, prove its protocols correct, and intelligently choose between Kafka, Pulsar, Pravega, Aeron, RedPanda, and WarpStream for a given problem." That requires three threads pulled in parallel: theory (Lynch, Cachin, Lamport, FLP, CAP, consensus, CRDTs), engineering (Akidau's *Streaming Systems*, Kleppmann's *DDIA*, Kafka and Flink source, the KIP archive), and practice (capstones, TLA+, OSS contributions, internal RFCs). The career payoff is real and bifurcated: the same skill set qualifies you for staff/principal interviews at FAANG-tier companies, for streaming-platform engineering at Confluent or LINE, and for the low-latency engineering teams that quant firms compete fiercely to staff.

Treat this document as a graduate syllabus, not a checklist. Skim it once, then read the section corresponding to the month you're in; treat the reading list as required, not suggested; and budget a non-trivial number of weekend hours for the capstones. The plan is intentionally too rich to complete in 12 months even at full intensity; the goal is to make every chosen path lead deeper rather than wider.

## Key Findings

A deep mastery program for EDA at the level you are targeting must rest on four legs that are usually presented separately and need to be braided together. The first leg is **distributed systems theory** taken from the primary literature rather than secondary blog posts: Lamport 1978, Fischer-Lynch-Paterson 1985, Chandy-Lamport 1985, Gilbert-Lynch 2002, Lamport's two Paxos papers, Ongaro-Ousterhout 2014 on Raft, Castro-Liskov 1999 on PBFT, the Spanner OSDI 2012 paper (and the more complete TOCS 31(3) 2013 version), the Calvin SIGMOD 2012 paper, and Shapiro et al.'s Inria RR-7506 on CRDTs. These are not optional. With a PhD in pure mathematics you will find them more comfortable than most engineers do; you should read them with the same rigor you would read a paper in your home field.

The second leg is **stream processing theory**, which essentially means reading Akidau, Chernyak, and Lax's *Streaming Systems* end-to-end alongside the two MillWheel and Dataflow papers, then applying the resulting "what / where / when / how" model to both Flink and Kafka Streams source code until you can reconstruct watermarks, triggers, and end-to-end exactly-once semantics from first principles. The third leg is **Kafka internals at engineering depth**, which means reading the broker source code, internalizing the KIP archive (especially KIP-500 for KRaft, KIP-405 for tiered storage, KIP-848 for the new consumer protocol, KIP-932 for share groups/queues, KIP-98 for transactions, and KIP-1150 for the diskless / object-storage architectural direction), and understanding the operational reality of running Kafka at scale. The fourth leg is **formal methods**: TLA+ taken seriously, including Lamport's video course and *Specifying Systems*, applied to your own protocols.

Crucially, the field has moved meaningfully in the last 24 months and your plan must reflect it. Kafka 4.0 (March 2025) shipped KRaft as the only consensus mode (ZooKeeper removed), KIP-848 as the GA consumer rebalance protocol, KIP-405 tiered storage as production-ready (the Apache Kafka 3.9.0 release announcement of November 6, 2024 states "Tiered storage is now production-ready in Kafka 3.9"; the KIP-405 wiki confirms "This KIP has been merged and marked production-ready since Kafka 3.9"), and KIP-932 share groups in early access — finalized in preview in 4.1, with Strimzi 0.46 supporting it from August 2025. KIP-1150 (diskless topics, accepted by the Apache Kafka community on March 2, 2026 with 9 binding votes) is shifting Kafka itself toward the WarpStream-style S3-native architecture, with competing KIPs (KIP-1176 fast-tiering for active segments, KIP-1183) representing alternative paths. Pulsar 4.0 and StreamNative continue to refine the segment-centric BookKeeper architecture. Flink 2.0 introduced disaggregated state management (Yuan Mei et al., VLDB 2025). Temporal has consolidated the durable-execution category, with Cadence now legacy. Treat any older curriculum you find online as out of date on these points.

## Details

### Part 1 — A Recommended Cadence (Months 1–18)

The shape of the plan is roughly: months 1–3 are theory and Kafka internals; months 4–6 are stream processing and Flink; months 7–9 are advanced patterns, sagas, schema governance, and the operational dimension; months 10–12 are HFT/low-latency and the first capstone; months 13–18 are deepening capstones, TLA+ work, OSS contribution, and a career sprint specific to your chosen path. The phases overlap and the reading list runs continuously underneath. The schedule assumes roughly ten to fifteen hours per week of focused study and project time, plus whatever you can fold into your day job.

Inside each phase, structure your week the same way: one paper or one book chapter on Sunday read with notes; two engineering sessions during the week (one reading source, one building); one writing session producing an artifact — a blog post, an internal RFC, a TLA+ spec, or a contribution patch. Producing artifacts is what converts reading into mastery; without it the material slides off.

### Part 2 — Distributed Systems Theory Foundations

You should approach distributed systems theory the way you would approach a graduate analysis class: build the canon from the primary papers, then triangulate with two textbook references — Nancy Lynch's *Distributed Algorithms* (Morgan Kaufmann, 1996) for the model-theoretic and impossibility flavor, and Christian Cachin, Rachid Guerraoui, and Luís Rodrigues's *Introduction to Reliable and Secure Distributed Programming*, second edition (Springer, 2011) for the modular, abstraction-driven flavor. Lynch is mathematically heavier and your background will reward you; Cachin is more pragmatic about real systems and pseudocode. Read both, but read Cachin first if you want to be productive sooner. A modern third companion is Maarten van Steen and Andrew Tanenbaum's *Distributed Systems*, third edition (van Steen, 2017, free PDF) for breadth.

The foundational papers are non-negotiable. Read **Lamport's "Time, Clocks, and the Ordering of Events in a Distributed System"** (*Communications of the ACM* 21(7), July 1978, DOI 10.1145/359545.359563) until you can rederive the happened-before relation and logical-clock construction from memory; this is the single highest-leverage paper in the field. Read **Fischer, Lynch, and Paterson's "Impossibility of Distributed Consensus with One Faulty Process"** (*Journal of the ACM* 32(2), April 1985) and convince yourself why the bivalence argument forces the result; FLP is the impossibility behind every consensus algorithm we use. Read **Chandy and Lamport's "Distributed Snapshots: Determining Global States of Distributed Systems"** (*ACM TOCS* 3(1), 1985) because every checkpointing mechanism in Flink, Spark Structured Streaming, and Pravega descends from it. Read **Gilbert and Lynch's "Brewer's Conjecture and the Feasibility of Consistent, Available, Partition-Tolerant Web Services"** (*SIGACT News* 33(2), 2002) for the formal CAP statement, and Brewer's own 2012 retrospective "CAP Twelve Years Later" (*IEEE Computer* 45(2)) to understand why "two of three" is misleading and why PACELC (Daniel Abadi, 2012) is the better operational model.

For consensus, read **Lamport's "The Part-Time Parliament"** (*ACM TOCS* 16(2), 1998) once for context and then **"Paxos Made Simple"** (*SIGACT News* 32(4), 2001) for the actual algorithm; multi-Paxos and its variants you will internalize through implementation. Read **Ongaro and Ousterhout's "In Search of an Understandable Consensus Algorithm"** (USENIX ATC 2014, Best Paper) and the extended tech report for Raft; ZAB you will see most clearly through the ZooKeeper paper (Hunt, Konar, Junqueira, Reed, USENIX ATC 2010); KRaft you will learn through KIP-500 and the Kafka source. Liskov and Cowling's "Viewstamped Replication Revisited" (MIT-CSAIL-TR-2012-021) is worth reading for completeness because it is essentially Raft's older sibling. Castro and Liskov's "Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance" (OSDI 1999) is the Byzantine baseline; Lamport, Shostak, and Pease's "The Byzantine Generals Problem" (*ACM TOPLAS* 4(3), 1982) gives you the 3f+1 lower bound.

Clocks and causality should be studied in a single block. Lamport 1978 gives you logical clocks; Colin Fidge (1988) and Friedemann Mattern (1988) independently introduced vector clocks; Sandeep Kulkarni et al. introduced **hybrid logical clocks (HLCs)** in 2014 ("Logical Physical Clocks and Consistent Snapshots in Globally Distributed Databases", which CockroachDB uses); Google's **TrueTime** is documented in the Spanner OSDI 2012 paper and most completely in the *ACM TOCS* 31(3) 2013 extended version, and is best understood as exposing clock uncertainty as a first-class API so that the database can wait it out at commit time. Spend a week absorbing why CockroachDB's HLC approach and Spanner's TrueTime approach reach the same external-consistency guarantee from different physical assumptions.

Consistency models in full rigor means starting with Herlihy and Wing's "Linearizability: A Correctness Condition for Concurrent Objects" (*ACM TOPLAS* 12(3), 1990), reading Bailis et al.'s "Highly Available Transactions: Virtues and Limitations" (VLDB 2014) and "Eventual Consistency Today" (*CACM* 56(5), 2013), and capping with the Jepsen consistency-model hierarchy on jepsen.io/consistency, which is the cleanest catalog of the lattice of consistency models in existence and which you should read as if it were a paper. Strong eventual consistency means reading **Shapiro, Preguiça, Baquero, and Zawirski's "A Comprehensive Study of Convergent and Commutative Replicated Data Types"** (Inria RR-7506, January 2011) and the SSS 2011 companion. Martin Kleppmann's CRDT line of work (the JSON CRDT paper in *IEEE TPDS* 28(10), 2017; the tree-move CRDT in *IEEE TPDS* 33(7), 2022; "Local-first software" with Wiggins, van Hardenberg, and McGranaghan at Onward! 2019; "Making CRDTs Byzantine fault tolerant" at PaPoC 2022) brings CRDTs into the present.

For distributed transactions, read the Gray and Lamport "Consensus on Transaction Commit" paper (*ACM TODS* 31(1), 2006) for Paxos Commit; understand 2PC and 3PC as the blocking and non-blocking baselines; and read **Thomson, Diamond, Weng, Ren, Shao, and Abadi's "Calvin: Fast Distributed Transactions for Partitioned Database Systems"** (SIGMOD 2012) for the deterministic-ordering alternative to Spanner's clock-based approach. Pat Helland's "Life Beyond Distributed Transactions: An Apostate's Opinion" (CIDR 2007) is the philosophical hinge between "ACID across services" and the saga/idempotent-operation worldview that underlies microservices and EDA.

You should hold yourself to a final test for this phase: can you state, without notes, what FLP rules out, why Paxos is not in contradiction with FLP, what Raft adds beyond Multi-Paxos, what linearizability requires that sequential consistency does not, what TrueTime exposes that NTP does not, why Calvin can avoid 2PC, and why CRDTs are coordination-free? If yes, you are ready to move to the engineering layers.

### Part 3 — Stream Processing Deep Mastery

The center of gravity is **Akidau, Chernyak, and Lax's *Streaming Systems: The What, Where, When, and How of Large-Scale Data Processing*** (O'Reilly, 2018, ISBN 9781491983874). Read it cover to cover, including the streams-and-tables half. Chapters 1–4 build the "what / where / when / how" framework: event time vs processing time, windowing, triggers, watermarks, and accumulation modes. Chapter 5 is the deep exactly-once chapter, much of which is co-author Reuven Lax's MillWheel and Dataflow work productized. Chapters 6–10 are the stream-and-table chapters that articulate Jay Kreps's "stream-table duality" and Pat Helland's "immutability changes everything" in concrete terms. Read it with a notebook; the diagrams will not stick otherwise. Read it twice. Akidau's "Streaming 101" and "Streaming 102" O'Reilly articles are the precursor and good as review.

Pair the book with the two foundational papers behind it: **Akidau et al., "MillWheel: Fault-Tolerant Stream Processing at Internet Scale"** (PVLDB 6(11), 2013) and **Akidau et al., "The Dataflow Model: A Practical Approach to Balancing Correctness, Latency, and Cost in Massive-Scale, Unbounded, Out-of-Order Data Processing"** (PVLDB 8(12), 2015). These are the intellectual seeds of Apache Beam and shaped Flink's semantics. Read them and trace the lineage to Beam's `PCollection`/`PTransform`/`Window`/`Trigger` model.

For Flink in depth, the books are Fabian Hueske and Vasiliki Kalavri's *Stream Processing with Apache Flink* (O'Reilly, 2019) and the official Flink docs (which are unusually good). Work through the DataStream API end-to-end, then the Table API and Flink SQL, then CEP, then **Stateful Functions** (now part of the Flink ecosystem). Read the disaggregated-state-management paper "Disaggregated State Management in Apache Flink® 2.0" (Yuan Mei et al., PVLDB 18, 2025) for the cutting edge. Watch the Flink Forward talks by **Stephan Ewen** (co-creator of Flink, formerly data Artisans / Ververica) and **Robert Metzger**. End-to-end exactly-once in Flink is the `TwoPhaseCommitSinkFunction` / `TwoPhaseCommittingSink` mechanism coordinating with Chandy-Lamport-style aligned checkpoints; read the 2018 Flink blog post "An Overview of End-to-End Exactly-Once Processing in Apache Flink (with Apache Kafka, too!)" by the Flink team for the canonical exposition, and then read the source of `KafkaSink` and `TwoPhaseCommittingSink`. RocksDB as the standard Flink state backend deserves a week of its own: read Facebook's RocksDB tuning guide on GitHub, the LSM-tree paper (O'Neil et al., 1996), and the RocksDB Universal Compaction notes; then read Flink's `EmbeddedRocksDBStateBackend` source.

For Kafka Streams advanced material, read the Confluent docs on the Processor API, custom state stores, interactive queries, and EOS v2; then read the source of the `StreamThread`, `TaskManager`, and `RecordCollector`. The EOS v2 design is essentially KIP-447 (producer per task → producer per thread, exploiting transactional producers across multiple partitions) and KIP-892 (transactional state stores). The "stream-table duality" (Kreps's "tables are streams accumulated over time; streams are the changelog of tables") is best internalized through Kreps's 2014 article "Why Local State is a Fundamental Primitive in Stream Processing" and his book *I Heart Logs* (O'Reilly, 2014, ~60 pages, read in an afternoon), then through implementing a `KTable` join from the Processor API.

The serving layer deserves its own week. **Materialize** (built by Frank McSherry and Arjun Narayan around the differential dataflow / timely dataflow line of research; read McSherry's 2013 "Differential Dataflow" paper) takes the "database inside out" view literally — streaming SQL with strict serializability and incremental view maintenance. **RisingWave** is the Rust-based, Postgres-compatible competitor; **ksqlDB** is Confluent's Kafka-Streams-on-SQL layer with materialized views. For real-time OLAP, study **Apache Pinot** (LinkedIn, real-time low-latency aggregation), **Apache Druid** (Metamarkets/Imply, segment-based time-series), and **ClickHouse** (Yandex, columnar OLAP that now dominates the open-source space). A useful exercise: build the same dashboard (clickstream sessionization with windowed counts) in Pinot, Druid, and ClickHouse and compare ingestion latency, query latency, and operational footprint.

Approximate algorithms in streaming are surprisingly underused. Read Flajolet et al.'s HyperLogLog paper (2007), Cormode and Muthukrishnan's Count-Min Sketch paper (2005), and Bloom's original 1970 paper; then implement each in Kotlin and slot them into a Kafka Streams topology for distinct-count and frequency-estimation on a high-cardinality clickstream. The book to cap this is *Mining of Massive Datasets* by Leskovec, Rajaraman, and Ullman (Cambridge, free online), especially chapters 4 (stream mining) and 6 (frequent itemsets).

For comparative perspective, study Apache Beam (the unified model that came out of Akidau's group; read the original Beam programming guide) and Spark Structured Streaming (Armbrust et al., "Structured Streaming: A Declarative API for Real-Time Applications in Apache Spark", SIGMOD 2018). Beam is intellectually clean; Spark Structured Streaming is pragmatically dominant in the lakehouse world. Both deserve a project each.

### Part 4 — Apache Kafka Internals at Engineering Depth

You will not be taken seriously by the Kafka platform community without reading source. Clone `apache/kafka` and budget six weeks. Start with the `clients/` module (the `KafkaProducer`, `KafkaConsumer`, and `Sender` classes), then the broker's network layer (`SocketServer`, `RequestChannel`, `Processor`, `KafkaApis`), then the log layer (`Log`, `LogSegment`, `LogCleaner` — note Kafka 4.x has moved much of this from Scala to Java under `storage/`), then replication (`ReplicaManager`, `ReplicaFetcherThread`, the ISR protocol), then the controller (`KafkaController` in ZK mode, `QuorumController` in KRaft mode). Stanislav Kozlovski's "2 Minute Streaming" newsletter (blog.2minutestreaming.com) and Jack Vanlightly's blog (jack-vanlightly.com) are the two best secondary sources; Vanlightly's "A Fork in the Road: Deciding Kafka's Diskless Future" (October 2025) is required reading on the KIP-1150 / KIP-1176 / KIP-1183 architectural debate.

The KIP archive is the project's design memory. Read the major KIPs in chronological order: KIP-32 (timestamps), KIP-98 (idempotent producer and transactions), KIP-101 / KIP-279 (leader epoch and unclean leader fixes), KIP-320 (offset reset and `OffsetForLeaderEpoch`), KIP-405 (tiered storage; per the Apache Kafka 3.9.0 release announcement of November 6, 2024, "Tiered storage is now production-ready in Kafka 3.9"; the KIP wiki confirms "This KIP has been merged and marked production-ready since Kafka 3.9"), KIP-447 (producer-per-thread for Streams EOS v2), KIP-500 (the KRaft replacement for ZooKeeper, GA in 3.3, default in 3.5, only mode in 4.0), KIP-516 (topic IDs), KIP-595 (Raft protocol), KIP-630 (snapshots), KIP-784 (consumer cooperative rebalancing improvements), KIP-848 (the new consumer rebalance protocol, GA in Kafka 4.0; per the Confluent blog post "KIP-848: A New Consumer Rebalance Protocol for Apache Kafka® 4.0", "Given the substantial benefits it provides, the new KIP-848 protocol will become the default rebalance protocol in a future Apache Kafka release, likely version 5.0"; it shifts coordination from clients to the broker-side group coordinator and uses incremental reconciliation via a continuous heartbeat), KIP-890 (transaction verification), KIP-892 (transactional state stores), KIP-932 (share groups / queues for Kafka, by Andrew Schofield, early access in 4.0 and preview-with-stable-record-format in 4.1, supported in Strimzi from version 0.46 in August 2025; introduces share groups that allow more consumers than partitions and per-message acknowledgment), and the KIP-1071 Streams Rebalance Protocol that brings KIP-848 semantics to Kafka Streams. Read each KIP twice: once for the design and once for the rejected alternatives.

KRaft consensus internals are the heart of modern Kafka. Read KIP-500, KIP-595 (Raft protocol design), and KIP-630 (Raft snapshots), then read the `raft/` and `metadata/` modules of the Kafka source. The KRaft implementation is a tailored Raft variant with batching and an event-driven controller; comparing it to the textbook Raft paper is instructive.

Tiered storage (KIP-405) decouples Kafka's compute from its primary storage by moving older log segments to an external object store (S3, GCS, HDFS) via the pluggable `RemoteStorageManager` and `RemoteLogMetadataManager` interfaces. Per the Kafka 4.1 operations docs, "the local tier is the same as the current Kafka that uses the local disks on the Kafka brokers to store the log segments. The new remote tier uses external storage systems, such as HDFS or S3." Compacted topics are not yet supported; JBOD is. Per Aiven's blog post "16 Ways Tiered Storage Makes Apache Kafka Better", tiered storage delivers "3-9x lower storage costs because object storage is simply a cheaper storage medium." KIP-1176 (fast-tiering active segments) and KIP-1183 are the next iterations.

Performance tuning means internalizing page cache behavior, `sendfile`-based zero-copy, the producer batching/`linger.ms`/compression trade-offs, the impact of partition count on metadata size and rebalance time, and the difference between `acks=1`, `acks=all`, and `min.insync.replicas`. Run benchmarks yourself with `kafka-producer-perf-test.sh` and `kafka-consumer-perf-test.sh`; do not rely on vendor numbers. Read Jun Rao's series of Confluent blog posts (especially "Optimizing Your Apache Kafka Deployment", "How to choose the number of topics/partitions in a Kafka cluster?", and the "Inside Confluent Cloud" posts on Kora).

The deployment-options comparison is now genuinely interesting and not a marketing question. Self-hosted Apache Kafka gives you maximum control and minimum cost-per-byte at sufficient scale, at the price of operational toil. **Confluent Cloud** runs on the Kora engine (multi-tenant, separation of storage and compute, autoscaling); **AWS MSK** is essentially managed open-source Kafka; **Aiven** is a multi-cloud managed offering; **RedPanda** is a C++ Kafka-protocol-compatible reimplementation with no JVM and a thread-per-core model based on Seastar; **WarpStream** (founded 2023 by Richard Artoul and Ryan Worl, acquired by Confluent in September 2024 and now part of IBM/Confluent's portfolio) is the diskless, S3-native architectural alternative — per its own architecture docs, "the Agent does not require local disks at all. Data streams directly from the Agent to object storage instead of being replicated via extremely expensive cross-AZ networking at an effective cost of $0.05/GB in AWS at retail prices." Per WarpStream's BYOC product page (warpstream.com/bring-your-own-cloud-kafka-data-streaming), "More than 80% of Kafka costs are not hardware – they're interzone networking fees." Latency is higher than self-hosted Kafka (~400–600 ms p99 produce on S3 Standard, single-digit ms on S3 Express One Zone). Per WarpStream's published case studies, named customers include **Pixel Federation** (saved 83% vs AWS MSK, from $60K/yr to under $10K/yr), **Goldsky** (TCO cut by 10×, over 90%), **Robinhood** (saved 45%), and **ShareChat** (saved 60%); WarpStream's ai-info page states that "Customers on standard cloud pricing consistently achieve 48–83% cost reductions." **AutoMQ** and Aiven's **Inkless** are competing S3-backed designs. The Apache Kafka community accepted KIP-1150 (Diskless Topics) on March 2, 2026 with 9 binding votes, validating the diskless architectural direction inside the upstream project itself. Spend a week understanding the trade-offs and form your own opinion.

| Option | Storage model | Operational model | Latency profile | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Apache Kafka self-hosted | Local disks + replication | DIY | <10 ms p99 | Sustained high throughput, full control |
| AWS MSK | Same as OSS Kafka | Managed control plane, you own tuning | Same as OSS | AWS-native organizations |
| Confluent Cloud (Kora) | Separated storage/compute | Fully managed, autoscaling | <10 ms p99 typical | Teams paying for support and features |
| RedPanda | Local NVMe, no JVM | DIY or BYOC | Lower p99 than JVM Kafka | Latency-sensitive within Kafka API |
| WarpStream | Direct to S3, stateless Agents | Fully managed control plane, BYOC | 400–600 ms p99 (S3 Std) | Cost-sensitive, latency-tolerant |
| Pulsar (StreamNative) | BookKeeper ledgers, segment-centric | Heavier ops footprint | Single-digit ms p99 | Multi-tenant + queue + stream blend |

### Part 5 — Distributed Logs and Storage Beyond Kafka

Once you can derive Kafka, study its competitors as serious design alternatives. **Apache Pulsar** (Yahoo, open-sourced 2016) decouples brokers (stateless message-routing layer) from storage via **Apache BookKeeper** and its segment-centric architecture: Pulsar topic partitions are split into ledgers that are striped across BookKeeper bookies, so a hot partition does not overload a fixed set of brokers, and brokers can be scaled independently from storage. This is the cleanest competing design philosophy to Kafka's and is worth a full month. Read the original BookKeeper paper, Sijie Guo's StreamNative blog posts (particularly the "Pulsar Newbie Guide for Kafka Engineers" series), and Jack Vanlightly's analyses. Note that Pulsar's heavier component count (brokers + bookies + ZooKeeper + RocksDB, or now optionally Oxia or etcd) is the trade-off for that architectural cleanliness.

**NATS JetStream** (Synadia) is the lightweight alternative; **RedPanda** (Vectorized) is the JVM-free Kafka-protocol-compatible alternative using Seastar's thread-per-core, future/promise model. **Pravega** (Dell/EMC) is the segment-oriented alternative most aligned with the long-term streaming-storage research agenda. Read each project's architecture page, run the quickstart, and produce a comparison table.

Differentiate event logs from message queues with precision. RabbitMQ, ActiveMQ Artemis, AWS SQS, and Google Cloud Pub/Sub are queues in the classical "competing consumers, per-message acknowledgement, no replay" sense. Kafka is an immutable log with consumer-group offset bookkeeping. KIP-932 (Queues for Kafka, by Andrew Schofield) introduces share groups that give Kafka queue-like semantics on top of its log — per Schofield's KIP and Gunnar Morling's "Let's Take a Look at... KIP-932" blog, a share group enables cooperative consumption with per-message acknowledgement and allows the number of consumers to exceed the number of partitions. This blurs the historical boundary; read Jack Vanlightly's blog post comparing queues and logs to internalize where each still has a structural advantage.

Distributed databases that double as event stores deserve a separate study block. **FoundationDB** (Apple) is the most architecturally interesting; read the SIGMOD 2021 paper and the FDB layer concept. **TiDB** (PingCAP) and **CockroachDB** (Cockroach Labs) are the production NewSQL options; both use Raft over Multi-Paxos-style range-based sharding. **Spanner** (Google Cloud) is the original. Read each architecture doc and run the tutorials.

Specialized event stores: **EventStoreDB** (Greg Young's company Event Store Ltd., now Kurrent) is the canonical event store with built-in projections and subscriptions; **Marten** (Jeremy Miller) is the Postgres-backed .NET event store; **Axon Server** is the Java/Spring-flavored one; Greg Young's *Versioning in an Event Sourced System* (Leanpub, ongoing) is the canonical reference text on event versioning. Per Michiel Overeem et al.'s empirical study "An empirical characterization of event sourced systems and their schema evolution — Lessons from industry" (*Science of Computer Programming*, 2021), the five empirically-observed schema-evolution tactics in production event-sourced systems are precisely **versioned events, weak schema, upcasting, in-place transformation, and copy-and-transform**. Each maps to operational choices: weak schema (tolerant readers, Postel's law) is the easiest in steady state but allows drift; upcasting middleware in the read path is the most production-friendly when versions accumulate; in-place transformation is dangerous; copy-and-transform (new stream, archive old) is the most expensive but cleanest for major schema changes.

Change data capture deserves its own deep dive. **Debezium** (Red Hat, led for years by Gunnar Morling) is the open-source standard; read the Debezium internals docs and Morling's blog at morling.dev, especially the "Five Advantages of Log-Based Change Data Capture" and the more recent posts on Debezium Server and Apache Iceberg sinks. Maxwell's Daemon is the lighter MySQL-only option. **AWS DMS** and **GCP Datastream** are the managed services. **ksqlDB CDC** is the Confluent-flavored option. The crucial distinction is log-based (read the database's redo/WAL log, e.g., MySQL binlog, Postgres logical replication) vs trigger-based (database triggers write to a shadow table) vs polling (periodic SELECT against a timestamp or version column). Log-based wins on every dimension except deployment friction, and modern operations are tractable enough that the trade-off almost always favors log-based.

### Part 6 — Advanced Patterns and Architectures

Sagas are the most misunderstood pattern in production EDA. Read Chris Richardson's *Microservices Patterns* (Manning, 2018) chapters 4 and 11 for the canonical orchestration-vs-choreography framing, then **Temporal** for the modern durable-execution approach. Temporal was founded in 2019 by **Maxim Fateev and Samar Abbas**, the original Cadence (Uber) and Azure Durable Functions technical leads. Temporal's value proposition, in its own technical writing, is that "every step of your workflow is persisted as an event in an Event History. If a worker process crashes halfway through a ten-step workflow, Temporal replays the event history on a new worker and resumes from exactly where it left off." This is, philosophically, event sourcing applied to control flow. **Cadence** (Uber, the predecessor) is now legacy and not the right choice for new projects per the Temporal team's public commentary. **Camunda 8 / Zeebe** is the BPMN-flavored alternative; **Netflix Conductor** (open-sourced 2016, now Orkes) is the Netflix-native option; **AWS Step Functions** is the AWS-managed option with limits on state size and execution duration. The right framework is decided by your tolerance for vendor lock-in, your team's BPMN familiarity, and whether your workflows are CPU-bound (Step Functions struggles) or long-running (Temporal shines).

Event sourcing in depth means going beyond "store events in a table" to address the hard parts: aggregate design (per Vaughn Vernon's *Implementing Domain-Driven Design*, Addison-Wesley 2013, and *Domain-Driven Design Distilled*, 2016), process managers and sagas as state machines, event versioning strategies (per Greg Young; per Overeem et al. above), snapshotting policies, and projection-rebuilding patterns. Snapshotting in event-sourced aggregates is an optimization (every N events, dump the rolled-up state); the event log remains the source of truth. Projection rebuilding requires a deterministic replay path and idempotent projectors; **CQRS** lets read models be rebuilt from scratch in a side environment and atomically swapped, which is the Netflix-style operational pattern.

Domain-driven design at the strategic level means reading Eric Evans's *Domain-Driven Design: Tackling Complexity in the Heart of Software* (Addison-Wesley, 2003) chapters on bounded contexts and context maps, Vaughn Vernon's *Implementing Domain-Driven Design* (2013), and **Alberto Brandolini's** *Introducing EventStorming* (Leanpub, ongoing) for the workshop methodology. Brandolini's "big-picture event storming" is, in my experience, the single most effective technique for getting a 20-person organization to agree on a domain model in two days. Run one with your team within the first six months of this plan.

The outbox pattern needs to be done correctly in production. The naive version (insert business row and outbox row in the same DB transaction, poll outbox table, write to Kafka) is correct but not idempotent and not ordered. The production version uses Debezium to stream the outbox table directly via CDC, includes a message-ID for deduplication on the consumer side, uses the aggregate ID as the Kafka key for partition-local ordering, and includes a `payload_id` watermark on the consumer for replay protection. Read Gunnar Morling's seminal Red Hat Developer blog post "Reliable Microservices Data Exchange with the Outbox Pattern" (2019) and his more recent KIP-1150 / "What If We Could Rebuild Kafka From Scratch?" posts (April 2025).

Event mesh products — **Solace PubSub+** (the original "event mesh" vendor), AWS **EventBridge**, and Confluent's **Stream Governance** with **Schema Registry** — are the inter-cluster, inter-organization layer above the broker. **AsyncAPI** (asyncapi.com) is the OpenAPI-equivalent specification for event-driven APIs; treat it as a first-class artifact for any externally-consumed event stream.

Schema evolution at organizational scale is where most EDA programs fail in year two. Read Adam Bellemare's *Building an Event-Driven Data Mesh* (O'Reilly, 2023) and *Building Event-Driven Microservices*, second edition (O'Reilly, revised) for the practical organizational playbook. Per Bellemare's January 2023 Confluent blog post "Data Contracts Are More Than Just APIs," the practical question is "why do data teams need data contracts whereas APIs suffice for everyone else?" — and the answer is that streams are denormalized, long-lived, and consumed by parties the producer cannot enumerate, so the contract must be explicit, versioned, validated at write-time, and enforced via Schema Registry with backward/forward-compatibility checks plus data-quality rules. Combine this with **Zhamak Dehghani's** *Data Mesh: Delivering Data-Driven Value at Scale* (O'Reilly, 2022) for the organizational principles: domain-driven ownership of data, data as a product, self-serve data infrastructure as a platform, and federated computational governance. The synthesis is what Bellemare calls an "event-driven data mesh" — event streams as the native interchange format between domain teams' data products.

### Part 7 — Operational Excellence and SRE for Streaming Systems

Running Kafka at scale is its own discipline. Capacity planning math is concrete: required brokers ≈ peak ingest (MB/s) × replication factor / per-broker network (MB/s), with headroom for replication catch-up and rebalances; disk = retention (s) × ingest × replication / compression ratio; partition count is dominated by consumer parallelism needs and per-partition metadata cost (each partition costs ~tens of KB in controller memory and a fetch RPC slot). The classic Confluent guidance on partition counts (Jun Rao's "How to choose the number of topics/partitions") is a few years old but still load-bearing; KIP-405 tiered storage and KIP-848 substantially relax the operational constraints that drove the historical "keep partitions per broker under 4000" rule.

Multi-tenancy means SASL/SCRAM or mTLS authentication, ACLs, and per-topic quotas. Encryption at rest is broker-side (file-system encryption, or the new KIP-317-style envelope encryption); encryption in transit is TLS. **Strimzi** (CNCF, the Kafka operator for Kubernetes) is the production-standard way to run Kafka on Kubernetes; read the Strimzi docs and Tomislav Vrbanec's and Paolo Patierno's blogs.

Cost optimization at scale is increasingly dominated by inter-AZ network egress. Per WarpStream's BYOC page, "more than 80% of Kafka costs are not hardware — they're interzone networking fees." Mitigations are rack-aware producers (KIP-392 follower fetching, KIP-881 rack-aware consumer partition assignment), compression tuning (zstd typically beats lz4 and snappy at acceptable CPU cost), and increasingly the diskless-topics direction (KIP-1150 / WarpStream / Inkless) for organizations that can tolerate ~hundreds-of-ms latency. Confluent's **Kora engine** in Confluent Cloud uses a different decomposition for the same goal.

Disaster recovery and multi-region: **MirrorMaker 2** is the open-source asynchronous replicator (KIP-382); it does not preserve offsets across clusters by default (it does maintain checkpoint topics for offset translation). **Confluent Cluster Linking** preserves offsets natively and is the recommended approach if you are on Confluent. Uber's **uReplicator** is the higher-scale custom alternative they built before MM2 matured; their engineering blog posts are still worth reading for the design trade-offs. Active-active multi-region is harder than it looks because of cross-region conflict resolution; in practice most organizations run active-passive with documented failover RPO/RTO and rehearse failovers quarterly.

Observability for event-driven systems requires three layers. Distributed tracing via **OpenTelemetry** with W3C TraceContext headers propagated through Kafka record headers (use the OTel Kafka instrumentation; do not roll your own). End-to-end metrics including consumer lag (the historical tool is LinkedIn's **Burrow**; the modern replacement is the lag metrics exposed natively in KIP-848 and surfaced in Confluent Control Center, Strimzi's metrics, and Prometheus). Correlation IDs in every event payload, propagated through the saga or workflow. Treat lag as the single most important SLI for a streaming system; SLO on p99 consumer lag for each consumer group and page on sustained breach.

Chaos engineering for streaming systems means testing broker loss (kill -9 a broker, watch ISR shrink and the controller elect a new leader), AZ loss (block all traffic from one AZ via `iptables`; watch producer retries and consumer rebalance), network partition (use Toxiproxy or `tc`), slow disk (use `cgroups` or `fio` to throttle), and producer/consumer poison-pill messages. The Jepsen tests on Kafka, RedPanda, and Pulsar are required reading; Kyle Kingsbury's Jepsen reports are how the distributed-systems community polices vendor correctness claims.

### Part 8 — Quantitative Finance and HFT Connections

Why event-driven thinking dominates HFT is structural, not aesthetic. Market data is by nature a stream of events (quotes, trades, order book updates); order entry is by nature a stream of commands; matching engines are by nature event-sourced state machines whose audit log is the regulatory record. The LMAX architecture is the cleanest public reference for how to build this in software. Read **Martin Fowler's "The LMAX Architecture"** (martinfowler.com, 2011) and the **LMAX Disruptor v1.0 technical paper** by Thompson, Farley, Barker, Gee, and Stewart (May 2011). Per the paper's abstract, the Disruptor achieves "over 25 million messages per second and latencies lower than 50 nanoseconds" on moderate-clock-rate processors; the canonical three-stage-pipeline microbenchmark measures ~52 ns mean per-hop latency against ~32,757 ns for `java.util.concurrent.ArrayBlockingQueue` — three orders of magnitude faster, and about 8× higher throughput in the same comparison. The mechanical-sympathy core ideas (ring buffer with pre-allocated entries, false-sharing avoidance via cache-line padding, single-writer principle, memory barriers instead of locks) are general and worth absorbing even if you never write trading code.

Exchange protocols form a thin but important sub-curriculum. **FIX (Financial Information eXchange)** is the IP of order routing, maintained by the FIX Trading Community (formerly FPL); per the FIX Trading Community's 2023 announcement, the live evolving standard is now "FIX Latest" updated via Extension Packs, having superseded the FIX 5.0 SP2 baseline. FIX 4.4 with errata (2003-06-18) remains the most widely deployed "classic" version for equities. FIXT.1.1 separates session and application layers. Implement a simple FIX 4.4 client in Kotlin against QuickFIX/J or QuickFIX-OSS and you will understand more about session, sequence numbers, and message types than three weeks of reading. **NASDAQ ITCH 5.0 (TotalView-ITCH)** is the outbound binary market-data feed carrying add/cancel/execute/trade messages with stock-locate integer codes and nanosecond-resolution timestamps; **NASDAQ OUCH 5.0** is the inbound order entry protocol; SoupBinTCP underlies OUCH for sequenced delivery. The specs are public PDFs on nasdaqtrader.com. Read them; parse a small captured market-data stream as an exercise.

**Aeron** (Real Logic; co-created by Martin Thompson and Todd Montgomery; now under the `aeron-io` GitHub organization since the team joined Adaptive Financial Consulting in 2022) is the contemporary low-latency messaging library. The Aeron transport delivers predictable single-digit-microsecond latencies for IPC and small UDP messages with throughput exceeding one million messages per second; **Aeron Cluster** is the Raft-based replicated-state-machine layer (Raft was chosen over Paxos for clarity and strictness, per Martin Thompson's QCon talk "Cluster Consensus: When Aeron Met Raft"); **SBE (Simple Binary Encoding)** is the OSI layer-6 zero-copy binary encoding library that pairs with Aeron. Per the Aeron.io site, Aeron Cluster delivers "throughput of over 1 million msg/sec with predictable latency as low as 18 microseconds on prem and sub 100 microseconds in the cloud." **Chronicle Software** (founded 2013 by Peter Lawrey, a Java Champion) ships **Chronicle Queue** (persisted, broker-less, memory-mapped, sub-100 ns inter-thread for Queue Zero), **Chronicle Wire** (text/binary serialization), **Chronicle Map**, and **Chronicle FIX**; per Chronicle's published materials, their microservices framework targets 30 µs end-to-end with 99% under 70 µs and the company services 8 of the top 11 investment banks.

Kernel-bypass and FPGA territory is the next layer down. **Solarflare/OpenOnload** (Solarflare acquired by Xilinx in 2019, AMD acquired Xilinx in 2022) provides userspace TCP/UDP via `LD_PRELOAD` plus the lower-level **ef_vi** layer-2 API for direct NIC access on Solarflare/AMD X2 series and SN1000 SmartNICs (FPGA-programmable); modern NIC hardware latency is in the 500–600 ns range with small payloads. **AMD TCPDirect** is the variant for ultra-low-latency TCP. **DPDK** (originally Intel, now Linux Foundation) is the standard polling-mode-driver kernel-bypass framework for market-data feed handlers. **AF_XDP** is the Linux-native intermediate option. **RDMA / InfiniBand / RoCE** is the standard for inter-host low-latency in colocation data centers. FPGA-based trading (Solarflare/AMD SN1000, Xilinx Alveo, Intel Stratix) is now mainstream in tier-one HFT shops for matching, market-data parsing, and risk checks; it is a 6-month sub-curriculum of its own if you wish to go there.

For time-series tick data, **kdb+/q** (KX Systems, originally Arthur Whitney) is the historical incumbent; the tick framework (TP/RDB/HDB) is roughly 34 lines of q code and worth implementing as an exercise. Whitney's q is an APL dialect, and your math background will make it easier than for most. The community 64-bit edition has been rebranded as **KDB-X** as of June 2025. Open-source alternatives include **QuestDB** (which per KX's own TSBS benchmark is the closest open-source competitor at roughly 3× slower than KDB-X on average, and faster on lastpoint / double-groupby workloads), **ClickHouse**, **TimescaleDB**, and **InfluxDB**. ArcticDB (Man Group's library) is worth a mention.

Latency tiers matter operationally. Sub-millisecond is the threshold where you must care about garbage collection, JIT warmup, network jitter, and process scheduling; microseconds is the threshold where you must care about cache misses, false sharing, and lock contention; nanoseconds is the threshold where you must care about pipeline depth, branch prediction, and physical wire length (literally — at ~30 cm/ns in fiber, two extra meters of cable is 6 ns). Sub-microsecond is FPGA territory.

The reading list for trading specifically: **Larry Harris**, *Trading and Exchanges: Market Microstructure for Practitioners* (Oxford, 2002, ISBN 9780195144703) — the canon; Harris held the Fred V. Keenan Chair at USC Marshall and was SEC Chief Economist 2002–2004. **Irene Aldridge**, *High-Frequency Trading: A Practical Guide to Algorithmic Strategies and Trading Systems*, 2nd ed. (Wiley, 2013, ISBN 9781118343500). **Rishi Narang**, *Inside the Black Box: A Simple Guide to Quantitative and High-Frequency Trading*, 2nd ed. (Wiley, 2013, ISBN 9781118362419). For market microstructure theory, Maureen O'Hara's *Market Microstructure Theory* (Blackwell, 1995) is the academic reference.

Firms that hire from a senior-EDA background into HFT and quant systems roles include Jane Street, Two Sigma, Citadel Securities, Hudson River Trading, Jump Trading, IMC, Optiver, DRW, Tower Research, XTX Markets, and on the bank side the e-trading desks at JPM, Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, and Deutsche Bank. The language stack varies dramatically: Jane Street is OCaml; Citadel and HRT are heavily C++; Optiver and IMC are Python over C++; XTX is heavy on KDB; Jump uses C++ and increasingly Rust. Aeron, SBE, and Chronicle are JVM-ecosystem entry points; if you want to position from a Kotlin/JVM background, the path is Aeron-Cluster + SBE + LMAX-style Disruptor patterns + a personal matching engine.

### Part 9 — Theoretical Computer Science Connections

Your PhD makes the formal-methods leg of this plan dramatically easier. **TLA+** is the one investment that pays off the most. Watch **Leslie Lamport's video course** (lamport.azurewebsites.net/video/videos.html) — about 12 lectures, roughly 4 hours total — including the Paxos Commit and Alternating Bit Protocol lectures. Then read Lamport's *Specifying Systems: The TLA+ Language and Tools for Hardware and Software Engineers* (Addison-Wesley, 2002, ISBN 0-321-14306-X). Then read Hillel Wayne's *Learn TLA+* (learntla.com, the free re-edition of his *Practical TLA+* book by Apress). The Amazon AWS paper "How Amazon Web Services Uses Formal Methods" (CACM 58(4), 2015, by Newcombe, Rath, Zhang, Munteanu, Brooker, Deardeuff) is the industrial validation; AWS used TLA+ to verify DynamoDB, S3, and Elastic Block Storage protocols and found bugs that would have taken months to surface in production.

Once you can write a basic TLA+ spec, specify a protocol you actually deploy. Start with the transactional outbox protocol (producer, broker, consumer, deduplication store), then a saga (workflow, compensations, retries, failure modes), then a leader-election protocol you have in production. The TLA+ examples GitHub repository (`tlaplus/Examples`) contains specs for Paxos, Raft, ZooKeeper Atomic Broadcast, and most named distributed protocols; read them as you would read papers. PlusCal is a higher-level pseudocode that compiles to TLA+ and is the right entry point for most engineering use cases. The TLAPS proof system is for when model checking is insufficient.

Process calculi are the more mathematical leg. **Hoare's CSP** (*Communicating Sequential Processes*, 1985, free online) and **Milner's π-calculus** (*Communicating and Mobile Systems: The π-Calculus*, 1999) are the foundational references; the **actor model** (Hewitt 1973; the canonical modern reference is Agha's *Actors: A Model of Concurrent Computation in Distributed Systems*, MIT 1986) is more directly applied in practice via Erlang/OTP and Akka. Petri nets (Reisig's *Understanding Petri Nets*, Springer 2013) are the natural formalism for modeling event flows with concurrency and conflict; BPMN and workflow engines like Camunda are essentially executable Petri-net dialects. **Linear temporal logic** (LTL) and its branching-time cousin CTL are the temporal-property languages you need to specify liveness and safety; TLA+'s temporal operators are LTL-derived.

The category-theory connection is real but easy to overstate. The clearest payoffs are **F-algebras for event-sourced aggregates** (an aggregate is the carrier of an F-algebra where F is the functor of possible events; folding the event stream is the unique homomorphism into the carrier — which is exactly what `apply(event)` does on an aggregate root) and **functors for stream transformations** (`map` is functorial, `flatMap` is monadic, `groupByKey` is a coend in the Kleisli category — none of this is required for production code but it does crystallize the design constraints). Bartosz Milewski's *Category Theory for Programmers* (free PDF) is the friendly introduction; Brendan Fong and David Spivak's *An Invitation to Applied Category Theory: Seven Sketches in Compositionality* (Cambridge, 2019) is the modern applied reference. Given your background you will likely find this material easy and pleasurable rather than essential.

### Part 12 — Capstone Projects

These are the artifacts that will distinguish you. Aim for four to six over the 18 months. Each is intentionally ambitious; the point is the depth of understanding produced, not whether the code reaches production maturity.

**Capstone 1 (months 3–5): A Kafka-protocol-compatible broker in Kotlin or Rust.** Implement the wire protocol (`ApiVersions`, `Metadata`, `Produce`, `Fetch`, `OffsetCommit`, `OffsetFetch`, `FindCoordinator`, `JoinGroup`/`SyncGroup`/`Heartbeat`). Store logs as memory-mapped append-only segment files with a separate `.index` (offset→position) and `.timeindex`. Implement a single-broker version first; then add the replication protocol (KIP-101 leader epoch correctly!) and a minimal KRaft-style controller. Make `kafka-console-producer.sh` and `kafka-console-consumer.sh` work against it. This is roughly two months of weekends and is the single best way to internalize the broker. The reference implementations to consult are Apache Kafka itself, RedPanda, and Sarama's protocol reverse-engineering docs.

**Capstone 2 (months 5–7): A distributed event store with Raft consensus from scratch.** Implement Raft (leader election, log replication, snapshotting, membership changes per Section 6 of Ongaro's thesis) in Kotlin. Layer an event-store API on top (append, read by stream, subscribe). Specify the protocol in TLA+ first and model-check it before writing code. Compare your implementation's behavior under Jepsen-style partition tests to the spec.

**Capstone 3 (months 7–9): A stream processing engine with watermarks and exactly-once semantics.** Implement a subset of the Akidau Dataflow model: bounded and unbounded sources, windowing (fixed, sliding, sessions), watermark propagation, triggers (early, on-time, late), accumulation modes (discarding, accumulating). Use the Chandy-Lamport algorithm for distributed snapshots and integrate a `TwoPhaseCommitSink` against your Capstone 1 broker. Validate semantics with the Beam validation test suite if you can wire it up.

**Capstone 4 (months 9–12): An HFT-style order book matching engine, event-sourced.** Implement a price-time-priority limit order book with `NEW`, `CANCEL`, `MODIFY`, and `MARKET` order types; an LMAX-style single-writer Disruptor in front; a journaler that writes events to disk and replays them on startup; and a FIX 4.4 acceptor (use QuickFIX/J) and/or a NASDAQ-OUCH-like binary protocol on the front. Benchmark throughput and end-to-end p99 latency. Aim for >100K orders/sec and <500 µs p99 on a developer laptop; you should be able to push to a million orders/sec on a tuned server. This capstone is what you walk into a quant-shop interview with.

**Capstone 5 (months 12–15): A meaningful contribution to Apache Kafka, Flink, Debezium, or Strimzi.** Target areas with active development: Kafka's KIP-848 client-side improvements (rack-aware strategies via KAFKA-17747 are still not yet supported per the Confluent KIP-848 blog), KIP-932 follow-ons (a more efficient share-group assignor than the early-access "assigns all available partitions to all subscribed group members" version is explicitly an open work item per the KIP-932 Early Access release notes), KIP-1071 Streams rebalance protocol completion, Debezium connectors for less-covered databases, Strimzi operator features. The path is: pick a "good first issue" in JIRA, get a small PR merged to learn the contribution flow, then pick a substantive issue you can spend two months on. **Capstone 6 (months 14–18): A complete event-driven SaaS application from scratch.** Pick a domain (e.g., a multi-tenant subscription billing platform; a multi-region content moderation pipeline; a logistics tracker). Build it end-to-end with: bounded contexts identified via an EventStorming session, an outbox-based CDC pipeline using Debezium, Confluent Schema Registry with data contracts, multi-region active-passive deployment with Cluster Linking or MirrorMaker 2, OpenTelemetry distributed tracing through Kafka headers, consumer-lag SLOs and on-call playbooks, a Temporal-orchestrated saga for the cross-aggregate workflows, and a TLA+ specification of the saga and the outbox. Deploy on Kubernetes with Strimzi. This is what you point at in a staff-engineer interview as the "tell me about a time you architected a system end-to-end" answer.

**Capstone 7 (continuous): TLA+ specifications.** Maintain a personal repository of TLA+ specs for every non-trivial distributed protocol you encounter or design. Start with the outbox, then the saga, then a leader-election protocol, then a sharded cache invalidation protocol. Aim for five specs over 18 months. Below is a fragment of what a minimal outbox spec looks like in PlusCal/TLA+, to anchor the practice:

```tla
---- MODULE TransactionalOutbox ----
EXTENDS Naturals, Sequences, TLC
CONSTANTS Messages
VARIABLES dbState, outbox, broker, consumed

Init == /\ dbState = << >>
        /\ outbox = << >>
        /\ broker = << >>
        /\ consumed = {}

WriteBusiness(m) ==
  /\ m \notin Range(dbState)
  /\ dbState' = Append(dbState, m)
  /\ outbox' = Append(outbox, m)         \* atomic: same DB transaction
  /\ UNCHANGED << broker, consumed >>

Publish ==
  /\ Len(outbox) > 0
  /\ broker' = Append(broker, Head(outbox))
  /\ outbox' = Tail(outbox)
  /\ UNCHANGED << dbState, consumed >>

Consume ==
  /\ Len(broker) > 0
  /\ consumed' = consumed \cup {Head(broker)}   \* idempotent set!
  /\ broker' = Tail(broker)
  /\ UNCHANGED << dbState, outbox >>

Next == \E m \in Messages: WriteBusiness(m) \/ Publish \/ Consume

EventualDelivery == <>[](Range(dbState) \subseteq consumed)
====
```

This is intentionally underspecified — the point is to do it, model-check it with TLC, and then refine. The same exercise for a saga with compensations is genuinely illuminating.

A representative Kotlin/Spring Boot snippet showing a production-quality outbox with idempotent consumption, suitable for your Aurora MySQL stack, is the kind of artifact you can produce daily:

```kotlin
@Transactional
fun reserveFunds(walletId: WalletId, amount: Money, idempotencyKey: UUID): Reservation {
    idempotencyRepo.lockOrInsert(idempotencyKey)
        ?: return reservationRepo.findByIdempotencyKey(idempotencyKey)!!
    val wallet = walletRepo.lockForUpdate(walletId)
    require(wallet.available >= amount) { "InsufficientFunds" }
    val reservation = wallet.reserve(amount)
    reservationRepo.save(reservation)
    outbox.append(
        aggregateId = walletId.value,
        type = "FundsReserved.v1",
        payload = FundsReservedV1(walletId, reservation.id, amount).toJson(),
        partitionKey = walletId.value     // ordering per wallet
    )
    return reservation
}
```

Debezium then streams `outbox_event` rows to a Kafka topic with `walletId` as the key, the consumer deduplicates on `(aggregateId, eventId)`, and you have correctness end-to-end with a clean rollback story.

### Part 13 — Career Path Specifics

Drive adoption by lowering activation energy: provide a starter template, a CI pipeline that validates schemas against the Schema Registry, an internal "EDA Office Hours" forty-five minutes a week. Handle resistance to async patterns with empathy and concrete trade-off tables; the most common objection ("debugging is harder") is real and the answer is OpenTelemetry traces propagated through Kafka headers plus correlation IDs. Mentor by pairing on the first three services that adopt the pattern. Your six-month, twelve-month, and eighteen-month milestones should be: an internal RFC ratified; a reference implementation in production; three teams shipping in the pattern; a measurable reduction in cross-team coupling or a measurable improvement in latency.

**Path B — Staff/Principal engineer interview prep.** The system-design topics that recur across FAANG and FAANG-adjacent loops are designing Twitter's feed, Uber's dispatch and surge pricing, Netflix's video streaming and recommendation pipeline, a payment system, a chat system (WhatsApp/Slack/Discord), a notification system, a rate limiter, a URL shortener, a distributed cache, a search system, and a feature store. Per recent Uber interview reports on LeetCode (2026), the SDE-2 / Staff loop typically includes one or two coding rounds (LC-medium / hard), one machine-coding round (a heavier 60-minute design-and-implement, e.g., "design a simplified in-memory Twitter"), one high-level system-design round (60–90 min, e.g., "design a notification system for a stock exchange" with Kafka vs RabbitMQ trade-offs, push vs pull, market-open thundering herds), and a hiring-manager round. At staff/principal, the system-design round shifts from "draw boxes" to "what are the four interesting trade-offs and which would you make"; the leadership round shifts to STAR-format ("conflict with senior engineer," "ambiguous strategic decision," "mentored someone through a hard situation"). Your preparation cadence: one full mock per week (use Pramp, Exponent's coaching, or a peer); a written one-page design for each of the canonical topics; the *Designing Data-Intensive Applications* reading review; the *Grokking the System Design Interview* and *System Design Interview Volume 1+2* (Alex Xu) decks; for leadership, the *Staff Engineer* and *The Staff Engineer's Path* books by Will Larson and Tanya Reilly. Budget 8–12 weeks of dedicated prep before you start applying.

**Path C — Streaming-platform team role.** These teams (Confluent's Kafka and Cloud teams; LinkedIn's Kafka team; LINE's Kafka platform team; Uber's streaming platform team; Coupang's data infrastructure; Netflix's Keystone; Pinterest's streaming; Stripe's data platform) do three things: build and operate multi-tenant Kafka/Flink/Pulsar clusters at petabyte scale; contribute upstream to Apache projects; build internal tooling (lag dashboards, deployment automation, schema governance UIs, cost attribution). To position for these roles, your portfolio should show: at least one merged Apache contribution, an operational blog series ("here's how I tuned a 100-broker cluster"), demonstrated TLA+ specification work, and ideally a conference talk. The interview process emphasizes deep Kafka internals ("walk me through what happens when a producer sends a record with `acks=all`"), operational scenarios ("a broker is consuming 90% CPU, walk me through your diagnosis"), and design ("design tiered storage; design a multi-region active-active deployment"). Apply when you have at least two of: the merged contribution, the blog series, the conference talk.

**Path D — Quantitative trading / HFT systems.** Event-driven backgrounds translate directly: matching engines are event-sourced state machines; risk engines are stream processors with windowing; back-testers are deterministic replay over event logs; market-data normalization is essentially CDC at very high rates. The low-latency engineering skills to develop, in roughly priority order, are: garbage-collection tuning on the JVM (or escape the JVM with Rust/C++), single-writer / lock-free data structures (the LMAX Disruptor patterns), mechanical sympathy (cache lines, NUMA, branch prediction), kernel-bypass networking (Aeron, Onload, DPDK), binary serialization (SBE, FlatBuffers, Cap'n Proto, manual layouts), and FPGA literacy if you want to push into the deepest tier. Firms hiring from this background include Jane Street (OCaml), Two Sigma (Python+C++), Citadel Securities (C++), Hudson River Trading (C++), Jump Trading (C++/Rust), IMC (C++/Python), Optiver (C++/Python), DRW, Tower Research, XTX Markets (kdb+), and the e-trading desks at Goldman, JPM, Morgan Stanley. Reading: Larry Harris, Aldridge, Narang, O'Hara as listed above; the LMAX paper and Martin Thompson's talks; the Aeron / SBE docs as practical references; the ITG and Tabb Group industry research notes. Your portfolio piece is Capstone 4 (the matching engine).

### Part 15 — Practice and Continuous Learning Routines

The habits are more important than the curriculum. **Daily**: 30 minutes of focused reading from the current Tier-1 book or paper, before email; 5 minutes of journaling (what did I learn, what am I confused about). **Weekly**: one paper read with notes (Sunday morning is the standard slot); one engineering session against the current capstone (Saturday); one writing session (Sunday afternoon) producing a blog post, ADR, RFC, or PR description; one community-engagement session (read the dev mailing-list digest, scan KIPs, scan the Confluent blog, read one engineering blog post from the rotating list). **Monthly**: a deeper retrospective — what's working, what isn't, what to drop. **Quarterly**: a portfolio review (is the capstone progressing? are the blog posts accumulating? is there an OSS contribution in flight?). **Annually**: revisit the career-path choice and adjust.

Paper reading clubs work when they have three to five committed people, a fixed weekly slot, a designated discussion lead per paper, and a "papers we love"-style format where you summarize, critique, and connect. If you cannot find five committed people, run a solo paper club: post a public summary of each paper to your blog within 48 hours of reading. The accountability of the public post substitutes for the social pressure of the group. The "Papers We Love" GitHub repository is the canonical curated list.

A personal lab matters. Run a 3-node Kafka cluster locally on Docker Compose or Kind; run Strimzi on a small kind cluster; run Flink in session mode; run Debezium against MySQL and Postgres; run Materialize or RisingWave against the Kafka cluster. Use it for every capstone and every experiment. Cost: a 32 GB-RAM workstation is sufficient. A small cloud account (AWS or GCP free tier, plus ~$50/month for an MSK or Confluent Cloud trial) extends it for multi-region experiments.

OSS contribution paths: start with documentation (every project has stale docs); then "good first issue" bugs; then features. The Strimzi project is particularly welcoming to new contributors and is a high-leverage way to build Kafka platform credibility. Debezium has a similar reputation. Apache Kafka itself has a higher bar but the project's mentorship is real once you have a non-trivial PR open.

Writing and blogging is the highest-leverage learning multiplier. Pick a platform (Medium, your own blog, dev.to, Substack) and commit to one post per month minimum. Topics: a paper summary; a capstone progress report; a deep dive on a KIP; an interview debrief; an opinion piece. The act of writing forces precision; the act of publishing exposes you to critique that surfaces gaps in your understanding. Within 12 months a consistent blog will be the single most important career artifact you own.

Mentoring others is the second-highest-leverage multiplier. Volunteer to mentor at least one junior engineer in EDA topics; teach an internal lunch-and-learn series on Kafka; contribute to your local meetup. The Feynman effect is real: you do not understand something until you have explained it to a smart person who is willing to push back.

## Recommendations

The single highest-leverage thing you can do in the next two weeks is to (1) order Akidau's *Streaming Systems* and Cachin et al.'s *Reliable and Secure Distributed Programming*; (2) clone `apache/kafka`, `apache/flink`, and `debezium/debezium`; (3) install the TLA+ Toolbox and watch Lamport's first three video lectures; (4) start a public blog with a one-paragraph "what I'm doing and why" post; and (5) pick Capstone 1 (the Kafka-protocol-compatible broker) as your first project. Begin reading Lamport 1978 in week one.

By month 3 you should have finished *Streaming Systems* and Cachin, read the first ten papers on the list, completed the broker capstone to MVP, and have a TLA+ specification of the transactional outbox. The benchmark that changes the plan is whether you are producing artifacts (blog posts, ADRs, PRs) at the cadence above; if not, reduce the reading load and increase the building load.

By month 6 you should be running a working Raft + event-store (Capstone 2) and have completed your first stream-processing capstone work. By month 9 the matching engine (Capstone 4) should be benchmarkable. By month 12 you should have one merged OSS contribution and have decided which career path to optimize for in months 13–18. The benchmark that triggers Path D specifically: if your matching engine sustains >500K orders/sec at <100 µs p99 on a tuned developer machine, you are competitive with mid-tier HFT firms' coding-screen bar and should start applying. The benchmark for Path C: one merged Apache contribution plus one conference-quality blog series. The benchmark for Path B: three consistent mock-interview offers at the staff level. The benchmark for Path A: an internal RFC ratified and three teams shipping against the reference implementation.

If you cannot maintain the cadence — which is genuinely demanding — drop the breadth aggressively and preserve the depth. The non-negotiable core is *Streaming Systems* + Cachin + Lamport's TLA+ + Capstones 1 and 4 + a consistent blog. Everything else is recoverable later. Better to finish two capstones deeply than to start six shallowly.

Finally, calibrate the career direction by 12 months, not by 6. The four paths are more complementary than they look — the matching engine impresses both quant firms and staff-engineer interviewers, the Apache contribution opens both streaming-platform doors and internal-architect credibility, the TLA+ specs impress both the formal-methods-curious quant teams and the rigorous-systems-engineering teams. Defer specialization until your portfolio is rich enough to make the conversation easy.

## Caveats

The plan as written is intentionally too rich for 12 months; treat it as 12–18 months minimum and 24 months realistic with a demanding day job. The Kafka source-reading exercise is large enough that some people will not finish it; that is acceptable if the KIP archive and the Vanlightly/Kozlovski blogs substitute. The HFT path is the most specialized and the most rewarding financially but it is genuinely hard to break into without a track record; the matching-engine capstone is necessary but possibly not sufficient.

Several specific facts in this plan are tied to the current state of the field and will age. KIP-848 is GA as of Kafka 4.0 (released March 2025) and per the Confluent blog announcement is targeted to become the default in Kafka 5.0; KIP-932 is in early access in 4.0 and preview in 4.1 (per the Apache Kafka cwiki, "Apache Kafka 4.1.0 is shipped with a preview release of KIP-932… not yet recommended for use on production clusters") with full record-format stability not finalized until later releases; KIP-405 has been production-ready since Kafka 3.9 (November 2024); KIP-1150 (Diskless Topics) was accepted by the Apache Kafka community on March 2, 2026, but the implementation is in flight and competing KIPs (KIP-1176, KIP-1183) represent alternative architectures whose ultimate resolution is not yet settled. Treat any 2026 source on these as the freshest authority. The WarpStream acquisition by Confluent (September 2024, with Confluent itself now a wholly-owned subsidiary of IBM as referenced in WarpStream's marketing) may reshape the vendor landscape further. Pulsar's positioning continues to evolve under StreamNative; Flink 2.0's disaggregated state management is still maturing.

The reading list is intentionally opinionated and some respected practitioners would substitute different choices. *Designing Data-Intensive Applications* is universally recommended but is now eight years old and predates KRaft, KIP-848, tiered storage, the data-mesh literature, and the Temporal/durable-execution consolidation; supplement with Bellemare and Dehghani and the recent KIP archive. Greg Young's event-sourcing work is canonical but he is a strong-opinions author and not everyone will agree with every position. The category-theory thread is genuinely optional for production work; do it if it brings you pleasure, skip it if not.

Finally, several quantitative claims in this plan are vendor-stated and should be reproduced before being cited in your own work. The LMAX numbers (over 25 million messages per second and <50 ns latency, with ~52 ns per-hop vs ~32,757 ns for ArrayBlockingQueue) are from the May 2011 v1.0 paper measured on then-moderate-clock-rate hardware in a specific three-stage-pipeline microbenchmark; modern measurements vary substantially. Aeron's quoted "sub-100 µs in the cloud, 18 µs on-prem" for Aeron Cluster comes from Real Logic's own materials. Chronicle's "30 µs end-to-end with 99% under 70 µs" and "8 of the top 11 investment banks" come from Chronicle Software's marketing. WarpStream's named customer savings (Pixel Federation 83%, Goldsky 10× / >90%, Robinhood 45%, ShareChat 60%) are sourced from WarpStream's own case-study pages; the umbrella "48–83% cost reductions" claim is from WarpStream's ai-info page. Aiven's "3–9× lower storage costs" claim for tiered storage is from their "16 Ways Tiered Storage Makes Apache Kafka Better" blog post. Always reproduce benchmarks yourself before relying on them in design decisions — this is the single most important habit the curriculum is trying to instill.
