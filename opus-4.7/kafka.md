# A staged path to Kafka mastery for a Kotlin/Spring Boot veteran

You are a senior backend engineer with a decade of distributed‑systems experience, so the goal of this plan is not to teach you what a topic is — it is to build, stage by stage, the deep model of Kafka that lets you reason about a cluster the way you already reason about Aurora and microservices. Kafka is deceptively simple on the surface (an append‑only log with consumer groups) and surprisingly subtle underneath (leader epochs, ISR semantics, transactional markers, cooperative rebalance state machines, RocksDB tuning). The plan below takes you from first principles through operations, stream processing, and architecture patterns, and finishes with specialization and community contribution. It is deliberately open‑ended on time: most strong engineers reach the "comfortable in production" level in three to four months of deliberate study and the "mastery" level in twelve to eighteen months with ongoing practice.

A single orienting fact before we start: **as of April 2026, Apache Kafka 4.2.0 is the current stable release**. Kafka 4.0 (March 2025) removed ZooKeeper entirely — KRaft is the only supported control plane. The next‑generation consumer rebalance protocol (KIP‑848) is GA and default‑eligible, share groups / queue semantics (KIP‑932) reached GA in 4.2, tiered storage (KIP‑405) has been GA since 3.9, and transaction hardening (KIP‑890 v2) is also GA. You should therefore learn Kafka in its KRaft form and treat any ZooKeeper content you encounter as historical. You will still meet ZK in the wild (Aurora‑era clusters, old books, many blog posts), so keep enough context to translate, but don't invest in it.

Everything below assumes a Kotlin codebase on a Mac M2, running brokers locally via Docker (Apple Silicon native images exist for `apache/kafka` and `confluentinc/cp-kafka` ≥ 7.5) and connecting to managed clusters (likely AWS MSK in `ap-northeast-2` and/or Confluent Cloud Seoul) for realistic scenarios.

## Stage 0 — Conceptual foundations before you touch a broker

**Why this stage matters.** Most Kafka failures in production are not bugs; they are consequences of a mental model that stops one layer too shallow. Before any code, spend a week internalizing the log abstraction and why it differs from a queue and from a database. This is the single highest‑leverage investment in the entire plan.

**Objectives and "done" criteria.** You can explain, without notes, why an append‑only log subsumes both messaging and database replication; you can draw the relationship between a partition, an offset, a consumer group, and a commit; you can articulate the CAP and consistency tradeoffs that Kafka makes versus Aurora's primary‑replica model; and you can state Kafka's durability guarantees in terms of `acks`, `min.insync.replicas`, and replication factor without hedging.

**Key concepts to internalize.** The log as a unifying abstraction; partitioning as the fundamental unit of parallelism; the difference between data replication (Kafka) and query replication (Aurora reader endpoints); ordering guarantees being per‑partition, never global; and the consumer group as a distributed cursor, not a subscription.

**Resources for this stage.** Read Jay Kreps' "The Log: What every software engineer should know about real‑time data's unifying abstraction" (linkedin.com/pulse, 2013) — it is dated in detail and timeless in framing. Read the first four chapters of Martin Kleppmann's **Designing Data‑Intensive Applications** (O'Reilly, 2017) on replication, partitioning, transactions and consistency, then Chapter 11 on stream processing; DDIA is the single best foundation for everything Kafka does. Watch Kleppmann's Strange Loop 2014 talk *"Turning the Database Inside‑Out"* on YouTube. Skim the free O'Reilly book **Designing Event‑Driven Systems** by Ben Stopford (~170 pages, free from Confluent) to get Ben's "central nervous system" mental model before it becomes a cliché in your head.

**Pitfalls.** Treating Kafka as "a better RabbitMQ" — it is not a queue, and mental models imported from JMS/AMQP will hurt you. Ignoring that *partition count is a one‑way door* for most topics. Assuming Kafka gives you global ordering (it doesn't — only per‑partition).

## Stage 1 — Kafka internals and the broker you will never see in production

**Why this stage matters.** You have fifteen years of Spring Boot ahead of you to practice producer/consumer code. You have exactly one chance to learn the broker well, and if you skip it you will be the engineer who cannot debug why the cluster is healthy but a single topic stalls.

**Objectives.** You can explain the lifecycle of a produce request from the client's `send()` to a leader acknowledgment, including network threads, request channel, request handler pool, log append, and replica fetch. You understand ISR mechanics well enough to answer "what happens if a follower lags?" without hedging. You can articulate why `acks=all` + `min.insync.replicas=2` + RF=3 is the canonical durable configuration, and what each one protects against. You can draw the KRaft controller quorum and describe how it differs from the old ZK‑based controller election.

**Concepts to internalize.** Broker architecture (network threads, IO threads, request purgatory); the controller (KRaft quorum using Raft over the `__cluster_metadata` topic); topics, partitions, and replica assignment; **ISR (in‑sync replicas)** defined by `replica.lag.time.max.ms`; **min.insync.replicas** as the durability floor; **unclean leader election** and why you keep it disabled; **Eligible Leader Replicas (ELR)** — default in 4.1+, a subset of ISR guaranteed up to the high watermark that supersedes "unclean or nothing" choices of the past; **log segments** and how they roll (`log.segment.bytes`, `log.roll.ms`); **retention** (time and size based) vs **compaction** (`cleanup.policy=compact` keeps latest value per key, with `min.cleanable.dirty.ratio`, `min.compaction.lag.ms`, `delete.retention.ms`); and the offset vocabulary — **High Watermark** (max offset replicated to all ISR, the consumer‑visible ceiling), **Log End Offset** (leader's latest append), **Last Stable Offset** (the transactional read ceiling under `read_committed`). Learn **leader epochs** (KIP‑101/320) — they are the mechanism that prevents stale writes after a flapping leader and come up in every interesting failure mode.

**Resources.** Read Part II of **Kafka: The Definitive Guide, 2nd edition** (Shapira, Palino, Sivaram, Petty; O'Reilly 2021) — the "Inside the Kafka Broker" and "Reliable Data Delivery" chapters are written by committers and are the ground truth, even where ZK appears (mentally substitute "KRaft controller"). Then do Jun Rao's free **Kafka Internals** course on developer.confluent.io — it is the deepest free material on the broker, taught by the co‑creator, and covers data/control plane, durability, compaction, transactions, tiered storage, geo‑replication, and elasticity. Follow that with **Jack Vanlightly's blog** at jack-vanlightly.com — his replication and KRaft deep dives (including the 2025 "Log Replication Disaggregation" and "Coordinated Progress" series) are the best independent treatment anywhere; his older "Reasoning about Kafka" posts are the clearest analysis of ISR edge cases you'll find. Skim the KIPs that matter operationally: KIP‑500 (KRaft), KIP‑966 (ELR), KIP‑405 (tiered storage), KIP‑848 (rebalance), KIP‑890 (transactions v2). You don't have to read all of them; read enough of one to see how a KIP is structured (motivation, public interface, rejected alternatives, test plan) so the others become skimmable.

**Tooling.** Install Kafka locally with `brew install kafka` (native arm64), but also run a three‑broker KRaft cluster in Docker Compose using `apache/kafka:4.2.0`. Use `kafka-topics.sh`, `kafka-configs.sh`, `kafka-log-dirs.sh`, and `kafka-dump-log.sh` to poke at segments directly. `kafka-dump-log.sh --files .../00000000000000000000.log --deep-iteration` will make log segments concrete. Install **kcat** (`brew install kcat`) as your universal probe.

**Hands‑on exercise.** Stand up a three‑broker KRaft cluster (Docker Compose on your M2), create a topic with RF=3 and `min.insync.replicas=2`, produce steady traffic, then kill one broker and observe ISR contraction via `kafka-topics.sh --describe`. Kill a second broker and watch `acks=all` producers start failing with `NotEnoughReplicasException` — this is the moment `min.insync.replicas` stops being an abstraction. Then bring a broker back, watch ISR heal, and use `kafka-dump-log.sh` to inspect a segment and see the leader epoch transitions. Write up a one‑page internal post explaining what you saw in terms of HW, ISR, and leader epoch. This write‑up is your artifact; it is worth more than a certificate.

**Pitfalls.** Mistaking `acks=all` for "all replicas" — it actually means "all members of the current ISR," which is why `min.insync.replicas` exists. Thinking you can shrink partition counts (you cannot, practically). Expecting the cluster to reject writes when a disk fills — it won't gracefully; plan capacity and monitor `kafka-log-dirs`.

## Stage 2 — Producers and consumers at the Kotlin level

**Why this stage matters.** Most of the subtle correctness bugs you will ship are in your producer configuration (partitioning, idempotence, ordering) and your consumer poll loop (commits, `max.poll.interval.ms`, rebalance handling). This is where to build muscle memory.

**Objectives.** You can hand‑write a Kotlin producer and consumer using the plain Apache Kafka client (no Spring), with correct error handling and manual commits, and defend every config choice. You can explain the difference between classic eager rebalance, cooperative sticky rebalance, and the new KIP‑848 protocol, and demonstrate each. You can reason about at‑least‑once, at‑most‑once, and exactly‑once semantics as consequences of configuration, not marketing categories.

**Concepts.** On the producer side, the record accumulator and sender thread; **`enable.idempotence=true` (default since 3.0)**, which implies `acks=all`, `retries=Integer.MAX_VALUE` bounded by `delivery.timeout.ms`, and `max.in.flight.requests.per.connection≤5` preserving ordering via per‑partition sequence numbers; **`batch.size`** (default 16 KB) and **`linger.ms`** (default 0) as the latency/throughput dial; compression — `compression.type` default is `none` (a common misconception is that zstd is default; it is not), and the practical choice is usually `lz4` for lowest CPU or `zstd` for best ratio on logs; `buffer.memory`, `max.block.ms`, and the `send()` back‑pressure model; and the partitioner — the default "sticky partitioner" in 2.4+ batches per‑partition rather than round‑robining each record. On the consumer side, the group coordinator; **rebalance protocols** — classic eager (stop the world, revoke all), cooperative‑sticky (incremental, since 2.4, opt‑in via `partition.assignment.strategy`), and the **new KIP‑848 consumer group protocol** (server‑side assignor, heartbeat‑based incremental reconciliation, opt‑in via `group.protocol=consumer`); the poll loop contract (`max.poll.records`, `max.poll.interval.ms` default 5 min — exceeding it triggers a rebalance), fetch sizing (`fetch.min.bytes`, `fetch.max.wait.ms`); `__consumer_offsets` as a compacted topic; auto vs manual commit and why auto‑commit silently causes at‑most‑once in failure modes; **static membership** (`group.instance.id`, KIP‑345) to avoid rebalances on rolling deploys; `ConsumerRebalanceListener` for draining partitions cleanly.

**Resources.** Read the producer and consumer chapters of **Kafka: The Definitive Guide, 2nd edition**, and for each config setting mentioned, look it up in the official Kafka 4.2 docs (kafka.apache.org/documentation). Take Stephane Maarek's *"Apache Kafka Series — Learn Apache Kafka for Beginners v3"* on Udemy (updated for 4.0 in Aug 2025) — it is a beginners' course in name only; the pacing is fine for experienced devs and his configuration explanations are genuinely careful. For rebalance protocols specifically, read Sophie Blee‑Goldman's Confluent blog posts on cooperative rebalancing, and the KIP‑848 design doc on the Apache wiki. For poll‑loop pitfalls, Gwen Shapira's old "Things I Wish I Knew" talks on YouTube are evergreen.

**Tooling.** The vanilla Apache Kafka client (`org.apache.kafka:kafka-clients:4.2.0`) used directly from Kotlin. Use JUnit 5 with **Testcontainers** (`org.testcontainers:kafka`) — on Mac M2, pin to `apache/kafka:4.2.0` which has native arm64 images; the `cp-kafka` images ≥ 7.5 also ship arm64. Use kcat to produce JSON from the shell and `kafka-consumer-groups.sh` to inspect groups, lag, and to reset offsets.

**Capstone for this stage.** Build a small Kotlin CLI tool — call it `kfk` — that wraps the AdminClient, a producer, and a consumer to reproduce kcat's most useful features plus lag reporting. Force yourself to handle cooperative rebalance events, implement a manual commit strategy with `commitSync` on partition revocation, and add a flag to toggle between classic and KIP‑848 protocols. When it works, deliberately break it: set `max.poll.interval.ms` to 10 seconds and `Thread.sleep(30000)` in the handler, and observe the rebalance; set `enable.idempotence=false` and prove duplicates under broker failure. The goal is to internalize failure modes in your own code.

**Pitfalls.** Catching `Exception` in the poll loop and swallowing rebalance exceptions. Using auto‑commit in code that does anything meaningful after `poll()`. Setting `max.in.flight.requests.per.connection` > 5 with idempotence on (the client will reject it; with idempotence off it silently breaks ordering on retry). Forgetting that `send()` returns a `Future` and not awaiting it before considering the message durable.

## Stage 3 — Spring for Apache Kafka and the Spring Boot integration you will actually ship

**Why this stage matters.** Your day job will be Spring Boot, not raw clients. Spring for Apache Kafka (`spring-kafka`) is opinionated in useful ways (listener containers, error handlers, retry topics, DLT, observation) and in occasionally confusing ways (ack modes, container lifecycle, transaction manager wiring). Getting this layer right determines whether your services are boring or fragile.

**Objectives.** You can scaffold a production‑ready Spring Boot 3.x/4.x + spring-kafka service in Kotlin that publishes, consumes, handles errors with non‑blocking retry and DLT, emits Micrometer observations, and tests cleanly with both `@EmbeddedKafka` and Testcontainers. You understand when to reach for Spring Cloud Stream versus plain spring-kafka, and when Reactor Kafka is worth the complexity.

**Concepts.** `KafkaTemplate` and its transactional variant; `ProducerFactory` / `ConsumerFactory` and why you sometimes want more than one; `@KafkaListener` with `ConcurrentKafkaListenerContainerFactory`, including `concurrency` (partitions per listener), `ackMode` (MANUAL, MANUAL_IMMEDIATE, RECORD, BATCH, TIME, COUNT, COUNT_TIME), and batch listeners; `DefaultErrorHandler` (which replaced the deprecated `SeekToCurrentErrorHandler`) with configurable `BackOff` and retry/non‑retry exception classification; `DeadLetterPublishingRecoverer` and how headers carry original topic/partition/offset/exception; **non‑blocking retry topics** via `@RetryableTopic`, which creates a chain of retry topics (e.g., `-retry-0`, `-retry-1`, … , `-dlt`) so slow failures do not block the consumer poll loop; Micrometer **Observation** integration (per‑record and, in 4.0+, per‑batch); Jackson 3 serializers in 4.0+ (`JacksonJsonSerializer` replacing `JsonSerializer`). For reactive stacks: Reactor Kafka (`io.projectreactor.kafka`) returning `Flux`/`Mono` and managing back‑pressure via poll pausing — useful with WebFlux but niche. For binder‑style abstractions: Spring Cloud Stream's functional model (`Function`, `Consumer`, `Supplier` beans; bindings like `orderProcessor-in-0`) and `StreamBridge`. Concretely: as of April 2026, **spring-kafka 4.0.4** is the current GA for Spring Boot 4.0, **3.3.14** is the mainstream GA for Spring Boot 3.5.x, and **4.1.0‑Mx** milestones target Kafka client 4.2.

**When to use which Spring abstraction.** Prefer plain **spring-kafka** for 90% of Kotlin microservices — you want direct access to `KafkaTemplate`, transactions, and headers. Reach for **Spring Cloud Stream** only if you need to stay broker‑agnostic across teams (Kafka + RabbitMQ + Kinesis) or if you're building a Kafka Streams pipeline and want Spring's functional binding for `KStream`. Use **Reactor Kafka** only if your service is already fully reactive; mixing reactive and imperative around the poll loop is a source of subtle bugs.

**Resources.** The official **Spring for Apache Kafka reference documentation** (docs.spring.io/spring-kafka/reference) is excellent and unusually well‑maintained — read it cover‑to‑cover once, then use it as reference. Josh Long's "Coffee + Software" videos and Gary Russell's blog posts (the primary spring-kafka committer) are the most reliable secondary sources. Confluent Developer's free **"Spring Framework and Apache Kafka"** course is a concise structured tour. For Spring Cloud Stream, Oleg Zhurakousky's posts and talks are authoritative. For error handling specifically, Tomasz Nurkiewicz and Gary Russell have strong content on retry topics vs DLQ.

**Tooling.** Gradle with the Kotlin DSL; `spring-boot-starter-kafka`; `spring-kafka-test` for `@EmbeddedKafka` (4.0+ is KRaft‑only; `kraft` attribute removed because it is always KRaft now); Testcontainers for full broker tests; kotest or JUnit 5 — your call. Run a local stack with Docker Compose: three Kafka brokers (KRaft), Schema Registry, **Kafbat UI** (`ghcr.io/kafbat/kafka-ui`, the actively‑maintained fork of the archived provectuslabs/kafka-ui), and optionally **Conduktor Console** or **Redpanda Console**. Note: Conduktor Desktop was retired end of 2025 — use Conduktor Console.

**Capstone for this stage — "OrderFlow v1".** Build the first iteration of a realistic order‑processing service in Kotlin + Spring Boot: an `order-api` that exposes a REST endpoint and publishes `OrderCreated` events to Kafka; an `order-fulfillment` service that consumes them, calls a fake payment provider (sometimes fails, sometimes slow), and publishes `OrderFulfilled` or `OrderFailed`; and a `notification` service that consumes terminal events. Implement non‑blocking `@RetryableTopic` with exponential backoff and a DLT; wire Micrometer observations to Prometheus with a Grafana dashboard; add integration tests with Testcontainers. Deploy locally with Docker Compose. This capstone will be extended throughout subsequent stages — treat it as your sandbox.

**Pitfalls.** Mixing transactional and non‑transactional `KafkaTemplate`s on the same producer factory. Forgetting that `concurrency` > partition count is wasted threads. Using blocking retries inside the listener for a downstream 10‑second outage — your consumer will be kicked out for exceeding `max.poll.interval.ms`. Assuming `@EmbeddedKafka` behaves identically to a real broker — it is close, but transaction timing and rebalance behavior can differ; use Testcontainers for anything touching transactions.

## Stage 4 — Schemas, Avro, and the contracts that keep distributed teams sane

**Why this stage matters.** JSON on Kafka is the gateway drug to production pain. The moment you have two teams producing and three teams consuming, you need a registry and a compatibility policy. This is the stage where you start designing systems, not just writing services.

**Objectives.** You can set up and operate a Schema Registry, author Avro/Protobuf schemas with explicit compatibility rules, reason about each compatibility mode (BACKWARD, BACKWARD_TRANSITIVE, FORWARD, FORWARD_TRANSITIVE, FULL, FULL_TRANSITIVE, NONE) in terms of who upgrades first, and handle breaking changes through topic versioning or dual‑write windows.

**Concepts.** Why schemas on the wire beat schemas in code comments; **Avro** (compact, schema‑required, good evolution rules) vs **Protobuf** (ubiquitous in polyglot orgs, slightly different evolution semantics — delete optional fields freely; `BACKWARD_TRANSITIVE` is a typical default because adding new message types isn't forward‑compatible) vs **JSON Schema** (weakest guarantees but sometimes politically inevitable). The Confluent wire format: a magic byte, a 4‑byte schema ID, then the payload; deserializers fetch the schema by ID and cache. **Subject naming strategies**: `TopicNameStrategy` (default; one schema per topic), `RecordNameStrategy` (one topic, many record types), `TopicRecordNameStrategy` (hybrid). Compatibility semantics in plain English: *BACKWARD* means a new consumer can read data written by the last producer schema (consumers upgrade first, safe changes: delete fields, add optional with default); *FORWARD* means the last consumer can read data written by the new producer schema (producers first, safe: add required fields, remove optional‑with‑default); *FULL* is both against the last version only; *_TRANSITIVE* variants check against *all* previous versions (much stricter, safer long‑term); *NONE* is a foot‑gun reserved for migrations. **Data contracts** and Confluent's CEL rules for field‑level validation and transformation — the 2024/2025 Stream Governance addition that turns Schema Registry into a real contract enforcement point.

**Registry options.** **Confluent Schema Registry** is the industry default and part of Confluent Cloud Stream Governance. **Apicurio Registry 3.x** is the fully OSS (Red Hat) alternative with broader payload support (Avro/Protobuf/JSON Schema/OpenAPI/AsyncAPI/GraphQL/WSDL), pluggable storage (KafkaSQL, Postgres, MSSQL), and Confluent‑API compatibility for drop‑in SerDes — good if you want to stay out of Confluent's ecosystem. **AWS Glue Schema Registry** integrates deeply with MSK, Kinesis, and IAM, and has a generous free tier — a strong choice for a Seoul/AWS‑native team.

**Resources.** The **Confluent Schema Registry documentation** is the canonical source. Take Confluent Developer's free **"Schema Registry 101"** and **"Governing Data Streams"** courses. Read Stephane Maarek's *"Confluent Schema Registry & REST Proxy — Hands On!"* course on Udemy for concrete walkthroughs. Adam Bellemare's **Building Event‑Driven Microservices** (O'Reilly 2020), Chapter 3 and the "schemas as contracts" thread throughout, is the best org‑level treatment. For Protobuf specifics, buf.build's documentation on backwards compatibility is excellent. Gunnar Morling's morling.dev has thoughtful posts on data contracts and schema evolution anti‑patterns.

**Tooling.** `io.confluent:kafka-avro-serializer` (and `-protobuf-serializer`, `-json-schema-serializer`); the Gradle `com.github.davidmc24.gradle.plugin.avro` plugin or Confluent's for code generation into Kotlin data classes; Schema Registry running locally in your Docker Compose stack.

**Capstone for this stage — "OrderFlow v2: Avro everywhere".** Migrate OrderFlow v1 to Avro with Schema Registry. Define `OrderCreated`, `OrderFulfilled`, and `OrderFailed` records with explicit versioning. Set compatibility to `BACKWARD_TRANSITIVE`. Now deliberately evolve: add an optional `discountCode` field (compatible — consumers unaffected); add a required `channel` field (breaking — prove it is rejected, then solve it via the "expand/contract" dual‑write migration: publish both v1 and v2 topics, migrate consumers, retire v1). Write contract tests that fail the CI build if a proposed schema change breaks compatibility against the registered version.

**Pitfalls.** Leaving compatibility on `NONE`. Confusing subject names with topic names and ending up with unmanageable schema sprawl. Shipping Protobuf schemas with `required` fields — they cannot be removed without breaking; use proto3 and optional semantics. Forgetting that Schema Registry is a stateful dependency — back up its `_schemas` topic.

## Stage 5 — Transactions and exactly‑once semantics done right

**Why this stage matters.** Everyone advertises exactly‑once; very few engineers can explain what it actually means. After this stage you should be one of them.

**Objectives.** You can define *idempotent production*, *Kafka transactions*, and *end‑to‑end exactly‑once* precisely. You can implement the read‑process‑write pattern in Kotlin + spring-kafka with transactions and prove with chaos testing that it deduplicates across failures. You can explain why EOS is a property of *the Kafka read‑process‑write loop*, not of writes to external systems, and know when to reach for the transactional outbox instead.

**Concepts.** The **idempotent producer** (on by default since 3.0): producer ID + per‑partition sequence numbers let the broker deduplicate retries. **Transactions**: a stable `transactional.id` (the fencing key — *must* be stable across restarts, typically derived from consumer group + partition assignment), `initTransactions`, `beginTransaction`, `send`, `sendOffsetsToTransaction`, `commitTransaction`/`abortTransaction`, and `transaction.timeout.ms` (default 60 s). The **transaction coordinator** and the `__transaction_state` topic. **Transaction markers** (commit/abort) written to each topic‑partition the transaction touched. **`isolation.level=read_committed`** on the consumer side: filters out records from aborted transactions and blocks reads past the **Last Stable Offset** until pending transactions resolve. **EOS v1 vs v2 (KIP‑447, since 2.5)**: v2 allows a single producer per Streams thread to span multiple input partitions — orders of magnitude cheaper than v1's producer‑per‑input‑partition. In Kafka Streams, `processing.guarantee=exactly_once` is deprecated; use **`exactly_once_v2`**. **KIP‑890 (GA in 4.0)** hardens transactions server‑side by bumping the producer epoch on every commit/abort, which eliminates the hanging‑transaction class of bugs that plagued earlier versions — enable Transaction Version 2 via the `transaction.version=2` feature flag (it auto‑enables when 4.0 metadata version is finalized).

**The critical insight.** Exactly‑once is end‑to‑end only when *everything is Kafka*. The moment you write to Aurora or call a REST endpoint, you have left the transactional boundary; idempotency or a transactional outbox is the right answer there, not `transactional.id`.

**Resources.** The **Confluent blog "Transactions in Apache Kafka"** (Apurva Mehta, Jason Gustafson) is the original reference and still the clearest. Jun Rao's Kafka Internals course has a solid transactions module. Read KIP‑98 (original transactions), KIP‑447 (EOS v2), and KIP‑890 (v2 server‑side hardening) — reading the KIPs is itself a skill. Chapter 8 of **Kafka: The Definitive Guide 2e** is the book treatment.

**Tooling.** spring-kafka's `KafkaTransactionManager` and `@Transactional` on your service methods; Testcontainers with deliberate broker kills mid‑transaction (using `toxiproxy` container to simulate network partitions) to prove EOS under chaos.

**Exercise.** Build a small "deduplicating aggregator" in Kotlin that reads `payment-attempts`, sums totals per customer, and writes `customer-balances`. Run it with `exactly_once_v2`, then chaos‑test: kill instances mid‑transaction, replay duplicates into the input topic, and prove the output has exactly the right balance. Keep this code — it is the reference you will come back to whenever a colleague claims EOS is impossible.

**Pitfalls.** Generating a random `transactional.id` on each startup — it breaks fencing and allows zombies. Assuming EOS extends to your Aurora write — it does not. Using EOS for high‑throughput bulk loads where the per‑transaction overhead dominates; sometimes at‑least‑once plus idempotent consumers is cheaper.

## Stage 6 — Kafka Streams in depth

**Why this stage matters.** Streams is where Kafka stops being a message bus and becomes a stream‑processing platform. The DSL looks like Java streams, which is misleading: the hard parts are the topology, state stores, repartitioning, and fault‑tolerance semantics.

**Objectives.** You can design a non‑trivial streaming topology (joins, windowed aggregations, stateful enrichment), explain where repartitioning happens, size and tune RocksDB state stores, use Interactive Queries to serve views, and test topologies deterministically with `TopologyTestDriver`.

**Concepts.** **KStream** (event stream), **KTable** (changelog — last value per key), **GlobalKTable** (fully replicated to each instance; no co‑partitioning needed for joins; ideal for slowly‑changing reference data). Stateless operations (`map`, `filter`, `flatMap`, `branch` which became `split().branch()`, `selectKey`, `mapValues` — prefer the latter when possible to avoid repartition). **Stateful operations** are the hard part: every `aggregate`/`reduce`/`count` uses a **state store** (default RocksDB, embedded off‑heap, with memory tuning via `RocksDBConfigSetter`), fault‑toleranced by a **compacted changelog topic** and optionally **standby replicas** (`num.standby.replicas` > 0 for warm failover). **Repartitioning** happens automatically when you change the key; it creates an internal topic. **Topology optimization** (`StreamsConfig.TOPOLOGY_OPTIMIZATION=all`) can eliminate redundant repartitions and reuse source topics as changelogs. **Windows**: tumbling (fixed, non‑overlapping), hopping (fixed, overlapping by advance), sliding (based on record‑time differences, since 2.7), session (inactivity gap). **Grace period** controls how long you accept late records; since 3.0 there is no retention fallback, so set grace explicitly. **Joins**: stream‑stream (windowed, co‑partitioned, default inner with `leftJoin`/`outerJoin`), stream‑table (non‑windowed lookup, co‑partitioned), table‑table (inner/left/outer, co‑partitioned), and **foreign‑key table‑table joins** (non‑co‑partitioned, since 2.4 — huge for microservice data denormalization). **Processor API** as the low‑level `Processor<KIn,VIn,KOut,VOut>` / `ProcessorContext` / state stores — the DSL compiles down to this, and you reach for it when the DSL cannot express punctuation, complex side effects, or custom stores. **Interactive Queries (IQv2)**: query the local state store via `KafkaStreams.store(...)` and `StoreQueryParameters`, and use `StreamsMetadata` to discover which instance owns a key for remote RPC. **Exactly‑once**: `processing.guarantee=exactly_once_v2`, with the transactional boundary covering input consume + state store update + output produce + offset commit. In Kafka 4.2, the new **Streams Rebalance Protocol (KIP‑1071)** reached GA — note the migration caveat (KAFKA‑20254): don't migrate existing classic‑protocol Streams groups in 4.2.0; new groups are fine.

**Resources.** **Mitch Seymour's "Mastering Kafka Streams and ksqlDB"** (O'Reilly 2021) is the best Streams book — project‑based, deep on state stores and testing. **William Bejeck's "Kafka Streams in Action, 2nd edition"** (Manning, 2024) is a strong complement — more reference‑style, covers Schema Registry, Connect, Spring Kafka integration. Pick Seymour as primary and Bejeck as reference, or vice versa. On developer.confluent.io, take **"Kafka Streams 101"** (free) and follow it with the intermediate Streams modules. Matthias J. Sax's and John Roesler's Kafka Summit talks on Streams internals are the best video content — search "Matthias Sax KStream" and "John Roesler state stores" on YouTube. Confluent's "Inside ksqlDB" course is worth a skim even though ksqlDB itself is de‑emphasized (see Stage 7).

**Tooling.** `org.apache.kafka:kafka-streams:4.2.0`; `kafka-streams-test-utils` for `TopologyTestDriver` (deterministic, in‑memory, no broker — essential for fast CI). For Kotlin, Kotlin DSL wrappers for Streams exist (e.g., `kotlin-kafka-streams-dsl`) but the Java DSL is perfectly usable; you may want to wrap common patterns in Kotlin extension functions rather than adopting a full wrapper.

**Capstone for this stage — "OrderFlow v3: Streams‑powered analytics".** Add a Kafka Streams service to OrderFlow that computes, per 1‑minute tumbling window, total order value per product category; joins orders with a `products` KTable (slowly‑changing, global) to enrich category; maintains an IQ‑queryable "top‑N customers last 5 minutes" store served via a REST endpoint; and publishes a `revenue-per-category` KTable downstream. Add `num.standby.replicas=1` and prove warm failover by killing an instance. Write all tests with `TopologyTestDriver`. Then run the whole thing with `exactly_once_v2` and chaos‑test.

**Pitfalls.** Chaining `map` when `mapValues` would do — you just triggered a repartition for no reason. Leaving `num.standby.replicas` at 0 and then being surprised by the cold‑start lag on failover. Forgetting that `TopologyTestDriver` runs synchronously with a fake wall clock — real brokers are messier. Sharing a single RocksDB memory pool across many stores without a `RocksDBConfigSetter` — Streams apps will happily consume all available RAM.

## Stage 7 — Kafka Connect, ksqlDB, and the data‑integration periphery

**Why this stage matters.** Most streaming systems are 20% stream processing and 80% moving data in and out. Connect is how serious teams avoid writing one‑off ETL services. Given you already work with Aurora MySQL, Debezium CDC is particularly high‑value.

**Objectives.** You can deploy a Kafka Connect cluster (distributed mode) with at least two connectors, understand converters, Single Message Transforms (SMTs), error handling, and DLQ in Connect, and implement CDC from Aurora MySQL to Kafka with Debezium, handling snapshots, schema changes, and outbox routing.

**Concepts.** Connect's **distributed mode** (workers form a cluster via Kafka topics `connect-configs`, `connect-offsets`, `connect-status`). **Source vs sink** connectors. **Converters** (AvroConverter, ProtobufConverter, JsonConverter with/without schemas, StringConverter) — converter choice is per‑worker or per‑connector and is separate from the underlying connector implementation. **SMTs** for lightweight per‑record transforms (RouteRegex, ExtractField, InsertField, ReplaceField, MaskField, Debezium's Outbox Event Router). **Error handling**: `errors.tolerance=all`, `errors.deadletterqueue.topic.name`, with original headers preserved. **Key connectors**: **Debezium** (3.x) for CDC — the MySQL connector works with Aurora MySQL but requires specific Aurora configuration (enable binary logging in the cluster parameter group with `binlog_format=ROW`, recommended `binlog_row_image=FULL` and `binlog_row_metadata=FULL`; grant `REPLICATION SLAVE, REPLICATION CLIENT, SELECT, RELOAD`; connect via the writer endpoint; be aware Aurora uses table‑level locks for initial snapshots because it doesn't allow the global read lock that Debezium prefers on vanilla MySQL). Confluent's managed MySQL CDC V1 reached EOL on March 31, 2026, so if you use Confluent Cloud, use V2. Other key connectors: JDBC Source/Sink (Confluent and Aiven OSS fork), S3 Sink (partitioned writes in Parquet/Avro/JSON), Elasticsearch Sink, MongoDB. **ksqlDB**'s current status: Confluent has visibly de‑prioritized it (raised CSU minimums in Confluent Cloud, strategic push toward Flink, community migrations away), and while Confluent Platform 8.2 still ships it, you should **not choose ksqlDB for new projects in 2026** — invest in Kafka Streams for embedded processing and **Apache Flink** (via Confluent Cloud for Apache Flink or AWS Managed Flink) for SQL‑style processing. Knowing ksqlDB for legacy systems is still useful.

**Resources.** Robin Moffatt's blog (rmoff.net) is the best practitioner source on Connect; search for his Debezium + MySQL and Debezium + Postgres walkthroughs. Gunnar Morling's morling.dev — he founded Debezium — for outbox, CDC patterns, and data‑contract thinking. Confluent Developer's free **"Kafka Connect 101"** and **"From Zero to Hero with Kafka Connect"** courses. Stephane Maarek's *Kafka Connect Hands On!* on Udemy. The Debezium documentation is unusually good; read the MySQL connector page end‑to‑end.

**Tooling.** Kafka Connect in distributed mode via Docker; Debezium MySQL connector; `connect-standalone` only for local toy exploration. **AWS MSK Connect** for managed Connect on AWS (IAM‑integrated, auto‑scaling) and **Confluent Cloud fully managed connectors** if you use Confluent Cloud — in both cases, the Debezium MySQL source is available as a managed option.

**Exercise.** Add a MySQL (or local Aurora‑compatible MySQL image) to your Docker stack, run Debezium MySQL CDC, produce changes to `customers` and `products` tables, and sink the Kafka streams into S3 (MinIO locally) with partitioning by date via the S3 Sink connector. Then implement the **outbox pattern**: in a Kotlin Spring Boot service, write both the business row and an `outbox_event` row in the same JDBC transaction; configure Debezium with the Outbox Event Router SMT to publish domain events to the correct topics. You now have exactly‑once delivery from a relational DB to Kafka — which is the real reason anyone runs CDC in production.

**Pitfalls.** Using Kafka Connect standalone mode for anything real — it has no fault tolerance. Picking `JsonConverter` with `schemas.enable=true` and then being surprised by 3× message size. Missing the `database.history.kafka.topic` setting on Debezium MySQL — the connector cannot recover without it. Forgetting that Debezium publishes schema‑change events on a separate topic and you need to think about their compatibility too.

## Stage 8 — Operations, monitoring, and capacity planning

**Why this stage matters.** You asked for "proficient in Kafka," not "comfortable with Spring Kafka." This stage is where the distinction shows. Every senior engineer who gets paged at 3 a.m. because of Kafka wishes they had done this stage sooner.

**Objectives.** You can size a cluster (partitions, brokers, storage) from first principles given a throughput target; you can draw the monitoring stack and name the top ten JMX metrics that matter; you can debug ISR shrinkage, rebalance storms, and consumer lag under load; you can design a multi‑region disaster‑recovery topology with MirrorMaker 2 or MSK Replicator; you understand rack awareness, quotas, and Cruise Control; and you can plan a rolling upgrade including KRaft controllers.

**Concepts.** **Sizing.** Target ~100 MB–1 GB per partition; keep partition counts per broker ≤ a few thousand (KRaft raises the ceiling dramatically vs ZK, but metadata overhead still matters); replication factor 3 and `min.insync.replicas=2` as defaults. **Partition count** is chosen by maximum parallel consumption needed (partitions ≥ max consumer instances) and by per‑partition throughput (a partition is a single‑consumer stream; if one consumer can't keep up, add partitions — but remember you can only increase, effectively never decrease). **Rack awareness**: set `broker.rack=<az>` on each broker so replica assignment spreads across AZs; enable **follower fetching** with `client.rack=<az>` on consumers (KIP‑392) to read from same‑AZ replicas and eliminate cross‑AZ read traffic — a large bill item on MSK. **Quotas** (producer/consumer byte rate, request rate) via `kafka-configs.sh` to protect shared clusters from noisy neighbors. **JMX metrics** that matter: `UnderReplicatedPartitions` (must be 0 in steady state), `UnderMinIsrPartitionCount` (0), `OfflinePartitionsCount` (0), `ActiveControllerCount` (exactly 1 cluster‑wide), `RequestHandlerAvgIdlePercent` and `NetworkProcessorAvgIdlePercent` (> 0.3 healthy, < 0.2 saturated), `BytesInPerSec` / `BytesOutPerSec` / `MessagesInPerSec`, `TotalTimeMs` per request type (produce/fetch consumer/fetch follower p99), `LogFlushRateAndTimeMs`, consumer `records-lag-max`, producer `record-error-rate` / `request-latency-avg` / `batch-size-avg` / `buffer-available-bytes`. KRaft‑specific: `MetadataLogLag`, controller idle ratio. **Common failure modes**: disk fill (monitor `kafka-log-dirs.sh --describe`); GC pauses (G1GC, 6–8 GB heap typical, the rest of RAM as page cache); controller failover storms; ISR shrinkage under load (usually `replica.fetch.*` tuning or network saturation); rebalance loops from long processing exceeding `max.poll.interval.ms`; unclean leader election → data loss (keep it disabled; ELR in 4.1+ gives you safer elections). **Cruise Control** (LinkedIn) automates partition rebalancing via goal‑based optimization (RackAware, ReplicaDistribution, DiskCapacity, NetworkInboundCapacity) with anomaly detection and self‑healing; essential on self‑managed clusters above ~10 brokers; integrated into Strimzi. **MirrorMaker 2** is Connect‑based replication with source/checkpoint/heartbeat topics; `DefaultReplicationPolicy` prefixes topic names with source alias (`us-east.orders`) while `IdentityReplicationPolicy` keeps names (usually what you want for DR failover); `sync.group.offsets.enabled=true` lets consumers fail over to the mirrored cluster; active‑active requires careful cycle prevention. On AWS, **MSK Replicator** is a managed MirrorMaker 2 alternative supporting Seoul and other regions. **Performance tuning summary**: producer — `batch.size` 64 KB–1 MB, `linger.ms` 5–20 ms, compression `lz4` or `zstd`; consumer — `fetch.min.bytes` up, tune `max.poll.records` so processing fits within `max.poll.interval.ms`; broker — `num.network.threads` ≈ CPU cores, `num.io.threads` ≈ 2× disks, `num.replica.fetchers` 4–8, G1GC with a modest heap; OS — **XFS preferred over ext4** (Kafka docs recommendation), `noatime`, `vm.swappiness=1`, large page cache, disable transparent hugepages, ulimit 100K+. **Benchmarking** with `kafka-producer-perf-test.sh` (4.2 added `--warmup-records`) and `kafka-consumer-perf-test.sh`. **Security**: TLS via `ssl.keystore.*` / `ssl.truststore.*`; SASL mechanisms PLAIN (only safe inside SASL_SSL), SCRAM‑SHA‑256/512 (creds in KRaft metadata now), GSSAPI (Kerberos, enterprise), OAUTHBEARER (OIDC/JWT; KIP‑1139 in 4.1 adds jwt‑bearer grant type to avoid secrets in config); ACLs via `kafka-acls.sh` with `StandardAuthorizer` in KRaft (replaces `AclAuthorizer`); default deny via `allow.everyone.if.no.acl.found=false`. **AWS MSK IAM auth** uses a custom SASL mechanism `AWS_MSK_IAM` via the `aws-msk-iam-auth` JAR — drop it on your classpath and Spring Boot picks it up.

**Resources.** The operations chapters (9–12) of **Kafka: The Definitive Guide 2e** are still the best single source. Jun Rao's Kafka Internals course covers elasticity, geo‑replication, and operations. Stephane Maarek's *Kafka Cluster Setup & Administration* and *Kafka Monitoring & Operations* Udemy courses are very practical. For monitoring specifically, the Confluent blog's long series on monitoring, the Datadog Kafka monitoring guide, and the `kafka_exporter` / JMX Exporter documentation are the right starting points. For MSK specifically, the **AWS MSK documentation** and the **AWS Big Data blog** Kafka posts are high quality. For deep understanding of Kafka's storage and replication, **Jack Vanlightly's blog** is unmatched. Conference talks: Todd Palino's "Running Kafka at Scale" lineage of talks (LinkedIn originally), Jason Gustafson on transactions internals, Colin McCabe on KRaft, Gwen Shapira on operations war stories.

**Certifications here.** This is when the **CCAAK (Confluent Certified Administrator for Apache Kafka)** is genuinely useful — $150, 90 min, ~55 questions, 2‑year validity; studying for it forces end‑to‑end operations coverage. The **CCDAK (Developer)** covers the dev side at the same price/format. As of April 2026 there is also **CCAC (Confluent Cloud Certified Operator)**, the refreshed cloud‑focused cert covering Cluster Linking, Stream Governance, managed connectors, and Flink on Confluent Cloud — only worth it if you operate Confluent Cloud professionally. **Are certs worth it for mastery?** The exams themselves carry modest job‑market signal outside Kafka‑heavy shops, but the *preparation* is one of the fastest ways to surface blind spots (Streams internals, Schema Registry compatibility modes, idempotence edge cases, MirrorMaker topic naming). If your goal is mastery and you don't mind $150, CCDAK followed by CCAAK is a disciplined structure; use Stephane Maarek's practice‑exam courses on Udemy as the legitimate paid prep (avoid any third‑party "dumps" sites — they violate the Confluent NDA and their accuracy is questionable).

**Tooling.** **JMX Exporter** + **Prometheus** + **Grafana** with a pre‑built Kafka dashboard (several good ones on grafana.com); **Cruise Control** locally via Docker to see rebalancing in action; **MirrorMaker 2** between two local clusters; **Strimzi** on `kind` or `minikube` if you want to see the Kubernetes operator pattern — Strimzi 0.51 (April 2026) is KRaft‑only, requires Kubernetes 1.30+, and uses `KafkaNodePool` CRs for broker/controller role separation.

**Capstone for this stage — "OrderFlow Ops".** Deploy your OrderFlow stack to a three‑AZ setup (locally: three Kafka brokers tagged with `broker.rack`, services bound to "AZs" via labels). Instrument everything with JMX Exporter → Prometheus → Grafana. Run a load test with `kafka-producer-perf-test` at 2×, 5×, 10× your baseline throughput; identify the first bottleneck (usually consumer lag from insufficient partitions or producer `batch.size`); tune and re‑run. Simulate failures: kill a broker (expect ISR shrink + recovery), fill a disk (expect partition unavailability), partition the network between brokers (expect controller re‑election). Set up MirrorMaker 2 replicating to a "DR" cluster with `IdentityReplicationPolicy` and offset sync enabled; practice a failover by pointing a consumer at the DR cluster and confirming it picks up near the right offset. Write a runbook: "When we see UnderReplicatedPartitions > 0 for > 5 minutes, we …". This runbook is more valuable than any certification.

**Pitfalls.** Monitoring `MessagesInPerSec` and thinking you have monitoring. Setting `unclean.leader.election.enable=true` to "improve availability" — you have chosen data loss. Assuming MSK Serverless is "just provisioned MSK without servers" — it has a 24‑hour retention cap and only supports IAM auth, which disqualifies it for many use cases. Forgetting that MirrorMaker 2 replicates data but not configuration (topics, ACLs) — use `sync.topic.configs.enabled` and manage ACLs separately.

## Stage 9 — Architecture patterns: event‑driven microservices the right way

**Why this stage matters.** The skills above let you ship a Kafka service. This stage lets you design an event‑driven system across teams without creating a distributed monolith.

**Objectives.** You can pick between event notification, event‑carried state transfer, and event sourcing for a given bounded context with justification; you can design a saga across four services in both choreography and orchestration styles and argue the tradeoffs; you can implement the transactional outbox pattern against Aurora MySQL with Debezium; and you can name the failure modes of each pattern (and the mitigations).

**Concepts and when to reach for each.** **Event notification** — thin events ("OrderCreated, id=123"), consumers call back for detail; low coupling in data, high coupling in runtime (every consumer needs your API up). **Event‑carried state transfer (ECST)** — events carry sufficient state for consumers to maintain local replicas; high autonomy, at the cost of larger events, schema governance, and compaction strategy. Use ECST for reference data and for cross‑team decoupling. **Event sourcing** — events are the source of truth per aggregate; state is derived by replay. Kafka can underpin event sourcing but isn't always ideal (per‑aggregate topics don't scale well; many teams use EventStoreDB or an RDBMS event table and propagate to Kafka). Reserve true event sourcing for domains where temporal queries and full history are first‑class requirements. **CQRS** — command side publishes events; read models are built by consumers into query‑optimized stores (Elasticsearch, Postgres read replicas, Redis). Kafka Streams is a natural fit for building the read models. **Transactional outbox with Debezium** — the single most reliable way to bridge Aurora MySQL and Kafka: your service writes a business row and an outbox row in one JDBC transaction; Debezium tails the MySQL binlog and publishes from the outbox. The Debezium Outbox Event Router SMT flattens outbox rows into domain events on the right topic. This is the canonical replacement for "write to DB, then write to Kafka" which has no atomicity. **Saga**: choreography (services react to events and emit the next — simple, but the end‑to‑end flow is implicit in the event graph) vs orchestration (a central orchestrator issues commands and listens for replies — clearer control flow and explicit compensations). Kafka supports both; orchestrators often live alongside Kafka in Temporal, Camunda, or a dedicated saga service. **Idempotent consumers** (track processed IDs, or use EOS read‑process‑write), **dead‑letter queues**, and the **retry topics pattern** (Uber's blog on it is the canonical source; spring-kafka's `@RetryableTopic` implements it). **Poison‑pill handling**: a record that always fails deserialization cannot be consumed past, so you need a deserialization exception handler that routes to a DLQ — Kafka Streams 4.2 added a built‑in DLQ for exception handlers.

**Resources.** **Chris Richardson, "Microservices Patterns"** (Manning, 2018) — chapters 4 (sagas), 5 (business logic with events), 6 (event sourcing), and 7 (querying with CQRS) are the pattern vocabulary you will use for the rest of your career. **Adam Bellemare, "Building Event‑Driven Microservices"** (O'Reilly, 2020) is the org‑level companion — data contracts, bounded contexts, event‑carried state transfer, team topologies. Bellemare's **"Building an Event‑Driven Data Mesh"** (O'Reilly, 2023) is the capstone read for when you are designing at the platform level. **Ben Stopford, "Designing Event‑Driven Systems"** (free) as the short architectural essay. Gunnar Morling's *"Reliable Microservices Data Exchange with the Outbox Pattern"* on the Debezium blog is the canonical outbox write‑up. For event sourcing specifically, Greg Young's talks on YouTube and Oskar Dudycz's "Event Modeling Anti‑Patterns" (Kafka Summit London 2024) are excellent. Confluent's *"Event Sourcing and CQRS with Kafka"* free course on developer.confluent.io. Martin Fowler's essays "What do you mean by 'Event‑Driven'?" and "Event Sourcing" are short and clarifying.

**Capstone for this stage — "OrderFlow v4: Full event‑driven redesign".** Redesign OrderFlow as a four‑service saga (order, payment, inventory, shipping). Implement it twice: once as choreography (each service subscribes to the previous step's terminal event), once as orchestration (an `order-orchestrator` service issues commands and listens for replies, with explicit compensation on failure). In each version, use the transactional outbox pattern against a local MySQL (Aurora‑compatible) for the services that own persistent state, and have Debezium route outbox events. Add idempotency keys so retries are safe. Build a read model with Kafka Streams ("customer order history") and expose it via Interactive Queries. Write a design doc comparing the two sagas — which failures does each handle well, which does each handle poorly, and when would you pick which.

**Pitfalls.** Choosing event sourcing because it sounds elegant, then discovering your team cannot reason about replays and migrations. Using choreography for an 8‑step workflow — orchestration exists for a reason. Dual‑writing (DB + Kafka) without outbox or transactional writes — one day one will succeed and the other won't, and debugging it is miserable. Treating retry topics as a panacea without thinking about ordering — if your domain needs strict per‑key order, bouncing records to a retry topic breaks it.

## Stage 10 — Managed Kafka, alternatives, and the strategic landscape

**Why this stage matters.** You will make platform decisions in the next five years. Kafka is not the only log, and not all Kafkas are equal. This stage makes you a credible voice in those decisions.

**Managed offerings with a Seoul (ap-northeast-2) lens.** **Confluent Cloud** is available in Seoul on AWS with Basic, Standard, Enterprise, Dedicated, and Freight (WarpStream‑like, cost‑optimized, relaxed latency) tiers; Stream Governance (Schema Registry, Data Contracts, Stream Catalog, Stream Lineage), Cluster Linking, and managed Flink are all in the region (mTLS on Enterprise is still "Limited Availability" in Seoul as of recent checks). Best pick if you want managed Flink + governance integrated. **AWS MSK** (Provisioned and Serverless) is well‑supported in Seoul; MSK Provisioned with ARM Graviton brokers (e.g., `kafka.m7g.large`) is cost‑efficient and IAM auth integrates natively with AWS workloads; **MSK Serverless** has a 24‑hour retention cap and requires IAM auth, which is fine for many event‑bus use cases but disqualifying for others; **MSK Connect** is managed Kafka Connect; **MSK Replicator** is managed MirrorMaker 2 for cross‑region and same‑region replication and works in Seoul. **Aiven for Apache Kafka** is multi‑cloud, all‑inclusive pricing (includes networking), and a strong fit if multi‑cloud portability matters; Seoul via AWS. **Redpanda Cloud** is available on AWS Seoul; Cloud Topics GA (April 2026) writes direct to S3. **WarpStream** was acquired by Confluent in September 2024 and is positioned as Confluent's BYOC tier (data plane in your VPC, control plane in Confluent Cloud), with a diskless direct‑to‑S3 architecture ideal for latency‑tolerant, log‑heavy workloads.

**Heuristic.** For a Kotlin/Spring Boot team in Seoul starting fresh: if you are AWS‑native and want the least friction, **MSK Provisioned with IAM auth on Graviton brokers** is the usual right answer; upgrade to **Confluent Cloud Enterprise in ap‑northeast‑2** when you want managed Flink and Stream Governance. Choose **Redpanda** if you are drawn to lower operational footprint (no JVM, no ZK-era baggage) and same wire protocol. Choose **WarpStream / Confluent Freight** when you are ingesting logs/observability/data‑lake feeds at scale and can tolerate ~1 s latency for dramatic cost reduction on cross‑AZ traffic. Avoid **Apache Pulsar** unless you specifically need its multi‑tenancy or native geo‑replication model — migration cost from Kafka is real and the ecosystem is thinner.

**Alternatives to know conceptually.** **Redpanda** — C++ single binary, thread‑per‑core, no JVM, Raft built‑in, Kafka API‑compatible, built‑in Schema Registry and HTTP proxy; tiered storage in the Enterprise edition; strong latency story (BSL license for Enterprise features). **Apache Pulsar** — two‑layer (brokers + BookKeeper), native multi‑tenancy, first‑class geo‑replication, tiered storage first‑class; not a drop‑in for Kafka (client/tooling differs); smaller ecosystem. **WarpStream** — stateless agents write directly to S3; all metadata in object store; diskless; Kafka‑protocol compatible; acquired by Confluent. **AutoMQ** — similar diskless pitch. These are worth understanding for the "diskless Kafka" conversation now dominating KIP discussions (KIP‑1150, KIP‑1176, KIP‑1183 compete on the direct‑to‑S3 topic design; read **Jack Vanlightly's October 2025 "A Fork in the Road" post** for the best analysis). The broader industry context — as Stanislav Kozlovski's December 2025 piece "Event Streaming is Topping Out" argues, streaming prices are deflating and the industry is consolidating around diskless architectures and Iceberg integration (Confluent's Tableflow is one manifestation). You should be aware of this; you should not let it make you cynical about Kafka — it remains the dominant platform by a very wide margin and all these innovations extend rather than replace it.

**Resources.** The respective product docs; **Jack Vanlightly's blog** for independent analysis of the diskless wave and Iceberg integration; **Gunnar Morling's "What If We Could Rebuild Kafka From Scratch?"** (April 2025) for the thought experiment behind KIP‑1150; **Stanislav Kozlovski's "2 Minute Streaming"** and **Big Data Stream** newsletters for strategic framing. For managed Kafka comparisons, the AWS, Confluent, and Aiven blogs each argue their own case — reading all three gives you the right triangulation.

The bar for good KIP participation is lower than you think. The KIPs you should be tracking in 2026: **KIP‑1150/1176/1183 (diskless Kafka)** — still converging; **KIP‑932 (Queues)** — now GA in 4.2, ecosystem is catching up; **KIP‑848 (consumer protocol)** — GA in 4.0, roll‑out patterns still being documented; **KIP‑405 (tiered storage)** — GA in 3.9, operational learnings still emerging.

**A suggested reading order for the key books**, consolidating the recommendations above: start in parallel with (1) **Kleppmann, Designing Data‑Intensive Applications** (2017; 2nd edition in progress but not shipped — the 1st edition remains current in April 2026) — read before/during everything; then (2) **Kafka: The Definitive Guide, 2nd ed** (Shapira et al., 2021) as your core text; (3) **Kafka in Action** (Scott, Gamov, Klein; Manning, 2022) as hands‑on reinforcement (skim if #2 felt solid); (4) **Designing Event‑Driven Systems** (Stopford, free) as the architectural essay; (5) **Microservices Patterns** (Richardson, 2018) for the pattern vocabulary; (6) **Building Event‑Driven Microservices** (Bellemare, 2020) for applied design; (7) **Mastering Kafka Streams and ksqlDB** (Seymour, O'Reilly 2021) as your Streams primary, or alternatively (8) **Kafka Streams in Action, 2nd ed** (Bejeck, Manning 2024) as primary; pick one of 7/8 and use the other as reference; (9) **Building an Event‑Driven Data Mesh** (Bellemare, 2023) as the platform‑level capstone. **Event Streams in Action** (Dean, Crettaz) is worth skipping unless you want cross‑tool breadth — it is aging.

## A note on tempo, and on finishing

You asked for deep mastery, not speed, so let the stages take the time they take. As a rough calibration: Stages 0–3 are a strong month of evening/weekend work for someone with your background; Stages 4–6 another two months; Stages 7–9 are two to three months because they involve substantial hands‑on work; Stages 10–11 are ongoing. The OrderFlow project is deliberately a single thread through the plan — it begins as a JSON REST service in Stage 3 and ends as a multi‑service, schema‑governed, exactly‑once, Streams‑powered, outbox‑bridged, multi‑region event‑driven system. That single project, built and rebuilt over the course of a year, will teach you more than any three books or four certifications.

One last framing: Kafka rewards engineers who treat the log itself as the interesting object — the replication, the offsets, the compacted history, the transactional markers. Once that object is as vivid in your head as a B‑tree is for Aurora, every higher‑level pattern (saga, CQRS, data mesh, event sourcing) becomes a question of "how do I compose operations on logs?" That is when you stop learning Kafka and start thinking in it.

Good luck. The community will be better for having you in it.
