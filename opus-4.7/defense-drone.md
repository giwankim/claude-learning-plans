# Defense drone engineering for a Seoul backend engineer

Your instinct is right: modern defense drone engineering is largely autonomous-driving plus adversarial radio, and the parts of it that look most like your day job — distributed sensor meshes, telemetry ingestion, command orchestration, DevSecOps — are arguably *the* strategic bottleneck in the industry right now, not a sideline. **Anduril's Lattice is a Rust-based, protobuf-over-gRPC, edge-to-cloud data fabric with an entity/task/object CQRS model; reading its public SDK feels uncannily like reading a Kafka+Spring architecture doc written by someone who also cares about DDIL links.** The math you already own (Lie groups, measure theory, convex optimization, game theory) maps one-to-one onto SE(3) state estimation, factor-graph SLAM, MPC, and adversarial RL — areas where a generalist SWE has no advantage over you. The realistic tension is geography: ITAR essentially closes US primes to a Korean national in Seoul, but the Korean ecosystem has moved in the last 18 months from "nothing interesting" to "three or four genuinely compelling options plus a credible Anduril-analog startup." Below is the full landscape, a concrete 12-month learning plan, and an honest decision framework.

## 1. The subsystem landscape of a modern military drone

A military UAV is best understood as a vertical stack: hard real-time flight control at the bottom, softer mission autonomy in the middle, a C2 fabric on top, and a communications spine running through all of it. Most layers have direct commercial-robotics analogs but carry a defense twist — contested RF, DO-178C-grade assurance, adversarial actors, and brutal SWaP-C (size, weight, power, cost) budgets.

**Flight control and GNC** is the innermost loop, stabilizing attitude and tracking trajectories at 100–1000 Hz. The two dominant open stacks are **PX4** (BSD, NuttX RTOS, the default in the Ukrainian FPV ecosystem and most Western prototypes) and **ArduPilot** (GPL, broader vehicle coverage). Both target the **Pixhawk Autopilot Bus** hardware standard and speak **MAVLink** to ground stations. On CCA-class aircraft — Anduril's Fury, Kratos XQ-58A, Shield AI's V-BAT — flight control is typically proprietary, DO-178C-style control-law code, often auto-generated from MATLAB/Simulink. The math is rigid-body dynamics with quaternion attitude, cascaded PID or LQR inner loops, and increasingly INDI (incremental nonlinear dynamic inversion) or L1 adaptive control for high-performance airframes. Navigation fuses IMU, magnetometer, barometer, GNSS, air-data, and visual-inertial odometry through an EKF (PX4's `ekf2` module is the canonical reference) or a factor-graph smoother (GTSAM). On contested battlefields GPS is jammed by default, so **GPS-denied navigation is a first-class requirement, not a fallback** — Skydio X10D uses pure VIO, Shield AI V-BAT advertises deep-penetration ISR in GNSS-denied environments, and Anduril's Pulsar actively spoofs adversary nav.

**The autonomy stack** — perception, SLAM, planning, decision-making — is the layer most cousin to autonomous driving. Perception runs EO/IR detection (YOLOv8/v10, RT-DETR, open-vocabulary detectors like Grounding DINO for long-tail ISR), multi-object tracking (ByteTrack, BoT-SORT, MOTR), and cross-modality ReID (EO↔IR↔SAR). State estimation uses visual-inertial SLAM (ORB-SLAM3, Kimera, VINS-Fusion) on small platforms, LiDAR-inertial (FAST-LIO) on heavier ones. Planning combines global search (A*, hybrid A*, RRT*) with local optimization (MPC, CHOMP, minimum-snap trajectories from Mellinger's UPenn GRASP lineage — and Adam Bry, Skydio's CEO, co-authored the differential-flatness work that underlies much of this). The decision layer in defense embeds **rules of engagement** and mission grammars: Shield AI's Hivemind is organized around "blocks" of re-composable perceive/decide/act behaviors scaling from Nova quadcopters through V-BAT to the F-16-based X-62A VISTA that flew DARPA ACE. Anduril's Lattice for Mission Autonomy inverts the traditional many-operators-per-platform ratio to one operator per many heterogeneous platforms.

**Embedded systems, RTOS, and firmware** partition into two compute domains on nearly every modern platform: a Pixhawk-class STM32 MCU running **PX4 on NuttX** or proprietary control-law code on **VxWorks 653** (ARINC 653 partitioned, DO-178C DAL A certified, used historically on F-35), plus a companion computer — typically an **NVIDIA Jetson Orin NX/Nano** or **Qualcomm Flight RB5 / QRB5165** — running embedded Linux with PREEMPT_RT for vision, autonomy, and ML. Hardware-in-the-loop rigs are essential: Shield AI's public account of the YFQ-44A Hivemind integration emphasizes "countless software-in-the-loop simulations and milestone hardware-in-the-loop test events." Firmware engineers here carry bare-metal STM32/ARM Cortex-M fluency, device-tree and kernel-module skills on the Linux side, and MISRA-C / DO-178C / ISO 26262 discipline.

**Computer vision and ML for automatic target recognition (ATR)** is the flagship AI problem of the field. The canonical government program is **Project Maven**, now a Program of Record under NGA/CDAO running on AWS, with a model hub including Claude and Llama variants and Palantir's Maven Smart System as the front end. MSS has **20,000+ active military users across INDOPACOM, EUCOM, CENTCOM, NORTHCOM, and NATO as of March 2026**. NGA director VADM Whitworth publicly stated Maven would begin transmitting "100% machine-generated" intelligence products to combatant commanders by June 2026 — treat that as a stated program goal, not a demonstrated capability. AeroVironment's Switchblade 600 Block 2 already integrates onboard ATR for terminal engagement.

**Communications is the subsystem that diverges hardest from autonomous driving.** Requirements include resilient C2 under jamming (frequency-hopping waveforms like L3Harris Wraith, DSSS, cognitive SDR); tactical mesh/MANET networking (Silvus StreamCaster, Persistent Systems Wave Relay as the de-facto physical meshes, Anduril's **Lattice Mesh** as the application-layer fabric above); tactical data links (Link 16 / MIDS, TTNT, increasingly open IP-over-tactical-radio); MUM-T (Manned-Unmanned Teaming) via the USAF's **Autonomy Government Reference Architecture (A-GRA)**, which in February 2026 enabled an in-flight swap between Anduril Lattice and Shield AI Hivemind on the YFQ-44A Fury; BLOS SATCOM; and GPS-denied nav using VIO, terrain-relative, celestial, or signals-of-opportunity (LEO constellations). This is where defense drones diverge hardest from Waymo — RF engineers, EW specialists, and protocol experts dominate here.

**Backend, cloud, and ground control** is your native habitat. The C2 tier ingests telemetry from thousands of platforms, fuses tracks, distributes tasks, and exposes operator UIs. The canonical products are **Anduril Lattice**, **Palantir Maven Smart System**, **ATAK/TAK** (the dismounted-warfighter client), plus ground stations like **QGroundControl** and **Mission Planner** for MAVLink platforms. A backend engineer with Kafka/Spring experience recognizes the ontology problem (entity model for tracks, assets, orders), the streaming problem (millions of sensor events per second at the edge), and the multi-tenancy problem (coalition and classification boundaries across IL-5/IL-6 enclaves). The differences: you run on **AWS GovCloud, Azure Government, Oracle Gov, or Palantir Apollo** rather than vanilla AWS; hard real-time constraints push logic to the tactical edge; and Kafka is rare *at* the edge — **DDS (RTI Connext), zenoh, and custom protobuf/gRPC fabrics dominate because of jitter and DDIL-link constraints**, though cloud-side is familiar Kafka/gRPC/Iceberg territory.

**Simulation and digital twins** are the multiplier that lets a company train and validate autonomy at scale. The current stack: **Gazebo Harmonic/Ionic** (the "Ignition" rebrand is done; Gazebo Classic went EOL January 2025) for PX4 SITL and ROS 2 work; **NVIDIA Isaac Sim 5.x + Isaac Lab** for GPU-parallel RL (Isaac Lab is the 2025 successor to the deprecated Isaac Gym); the **NVIDIA Cosmos** world-foundation-model family (Cosmos-Predict2.5, Transfer2.5, Reason2) for diffusion-based photorealistic domain randomization; and Unreal-Engine-5-based bespoke sims. Microsoft archived AirSim in July 2022 — the actively maintained successor is **Cosys-AirSim** (University of Antwerp, UE5.5). **Shield AI acquired Aechelon Technology in 2025 explicitly to build a "Hivemind Foundation Model for Defense,"** a direct analog of the Tesla/Waymo data engine.

**Systems engineering, integration, and safety-critical software** are what actually ship product in defense. DOORS for requirements, ICDs for interfaces, MIL-STD-810 for environmental qualification, MIL-STD-461 for EMI, FMEA, V-model development. The software assurance standards to memorize are **DO-178C** (five Design Assurance Levels; Level A requires MC/DC coverage and independent verification, and VxWorks 653 + RTI Connext DDS Cert are pre-qualified to DAL A), **DO-254** for complex airborne hardware, **DO-326A** for airworthiness security, **MISRA C/C++** for safe subsets with static analysis (PC-lint Plus, Polyspace, Coverity), and **MIL-STD-882E** for system safety. The interesting architectural trick in the CCA ecosystem is partitioning: a certifiable DAL-A core for flight-critical functions, paired with a "mission computer" running looser-assurance autonomy and ML — this is how Anduril, Shield AI, and General Atomics reconcile Silicon-Valley iteration speed with airworthiness.

## 3. Autonomous driving overlap is real, but the edges are sharper than you think

Your intuition is largely correct at the algorithmic layer. **Perception pipelines** (camera/LiDAR/radar fusion, early-fusion BEV transformers, YOLO/DETR detectors, ByteTrack), **state estimation and SLAM** (VIO, LIO, factor graphs on SE(3)), **planning** (A*, hybrid-A*, RRT*, MPC, iLQR — and Mellinger minimum-snap and Richter-Bry differential-flatness trajectories generalize from quadrotors to ground robots directly), **behavior prediction** (Trajectron++, transformer motion forecasters), and **sim-to-real data engines** (procedural scene generation, long-tail mining, active-learning triage loops) are algorithmically near-identical between Waymo and Anduril. Waymo's EMMA / end-to-end foundation driver and Wayve's pure-transformer driver are architecturally indistinguishable from Shield AI's "Hivemind Foundation Model for Defense" direction of travel.

What is genuinely different is the edge. An AV encounters an adversarial pedestrian maybe once a year; a CCA expects the environment to *actively try to kill it*. The threat model shifts from "robust to noise" to "robust to an intelligent, resourced adversary" — jamming, spoofing, SAMs, directed-energy weapons, adversary CCAs. AVs assume GNSS is always available; military drones assume GNSS is denied and architect around VIO, terrain-relative nav, and signals-of-opportunity as primary. AV safety is about preventing collisions; defense autonomy also cares about **positive target identification, collateral-damage estimation, human-on-the-loop engagement authority, and law-of-armed-conflict compliance** — Bob Work's "autonomy at rest" versus "autonomy on the move" distinction is a live policy debate, and Replicator is reportedly moving toward the latter. AVs are single-agent planners in shared infrastructure; **DARPA OFFSET** (2017-2021) targeted 250+ heterogeneous UAS/UGS in urban swarms, and Shield AI's "one operator to ten V-BATs" is a multi-agent MARL problem, not a single-vehicle one. RF and EW are first-class layers in defense (Pulsar, Wraith, cognitive radio), absent in AV. SWaP-C is brutally tighter — a small loitering munition runs on **10–50 W of compute budget** versus kilowatts in an AV, forcing aggressive INT8/INT4 quantization and custom accelerators. And drones are full 6-DoF with strong rotational-translational coupling, changing trajectory optimization and collision avoidance versus 2.5D AVs.

The talent flow is accelerating and well-documented. **April 2026 TechCrunch Mobility reporting: defense-tech startups are now the highest payers in the broader robotics market, with $300K–$500K base salaries for AV-adjacent engineers** — "defense tech startups are apparently the most generous… thanks to the Department of Defense's open wallet." **The Cruise shutdown in December 2024** (GM wound down the robotaxi business) and the **Argo AI collapse in 2022** released large cohorts of senior AV engineers into Anduril, Shield AI, Skydio, and Saronic. **Skydio's founding team (Adam Bry, Abe Bachrach, Matt Donahoe) came directly from the MIT DARPA Urban Challenge team and Google X's Project Wing** — the AV-to-drone pathway is literally how Skydio was built; Bry discusses this lineage on Shawn Ryan Show #258 (December 2025) and on MIT LIDS talks. **Heron Systems**, the DARPA AlphaDogfight Trials winner whose deep-RL agent beat a USAF Weapons Instructor 5-0, was acquired by **Shield AI in 2021** — its air-combat RL became the kernel of Hivemind's dogfight behaviors. **Archer Aviation raised $430M for a defense business unit with an exclusive Anduril partnership**, pulling eVTOL/AV aerospace engineers into defense.

## 4. Where your backend and distributed-systems experience actually plugs in

**Lattice is, architecturally, a protobuf-and-gRPC CQRS system with an edge overlay.** Read that sentence again if it helps. The Lattice SDK (publicly released December 2024 at `developer.anduril.com` under CDAO's Open DAGIR initiative) exposes three core APIs: **Entities** (publish/subscribe anything operationally relevant — sensors, tracks, platforms, AOIs — as typed, versioned, TTL'd documents), **Tasks** (sequential command routing to agents with bidirectional status streams), and **Objects** (content-addressed distributed binary blob store for imagery, video, MCAP logs, model weights). Protocol Buffers are managed through the **Buf Schema Registry**, gRPC bidirectional streaming handles real-time tracks, and REST mirrors handle non-latency-critical integrations. SDK clients exist in Python, Go, Java, JavaScript, C++, and Rust (the Rust SDK was archived July 2025 in favor of direct Buf generation). SVP Thomas Keane's framing — *"remove Anduril as any form of bottleneck… allow anyone to publish to the Lattice Mesh"* — is the strategic reason this matters.

Every deployment runs a **Lattice node**, a Rust process that speaks gRPC/protobuf peer-to-peer and hosts pluggable "translator" adapters for each sensor or effector. **Over 100 sensor types are integrated as of late 2024, with new ones added weekly.** Nodes exchange entities, tasks, and objects through a peer-to-peer mesh with store-and-forward behavior over DDIL links — no hub-and-spoke dependency. The same runtime (scaled differently) executes from Jetson-Orin-class edge boxes to Azure Government and AWS GovCloud regions. **NixOS is the edge deployment substrate. Azure Government became the preferred hyperscale cloud after the February 2025 Microsoft–Anduril IVAS agreement.** Anduril has not publicly confirmed whether the autonomy runtime uses behavior trees, hierarchical task networks, or mission graphs, but the A-GRA architecture and the February 2026 in-flight Hivemind-to-Lattice swap on the YFQ-44A tell us the autonomy runtime is **swappable as an app layer on top of a standard airframe interface**.

Your Kotlin/Spring/Kafka/AWS skill set maps onto defense drone engineering in roughly this priority order:

1. **DevSecOps for defense environments** — AWS GovCloud (IL2/IL4), Secret (IL5), Top Secret (IL6), Azure Government, Platform One, the Iron Bank hardened container registry, the Big Bang reference stack (Istio, Kyverno, ArgoCD, Gitea, SonarQube), Repo One source, Party Bus managed K8s, RMF/NIST 800-53 compliance, cATO for continuous authorization, and FedRAMP High. **Kotlin/Spring Boot services deploy into Big Bang via Iron Bank images cleanly**, and many DoD teams use exactly that stack. This is the natural landing spot for your profile.
2. **C2 and ground-control backends** — Lattice third-party apps, Palantir Gotham/AIP integrations, TAK/ATAK server-side, custom GCS software. CQRS, event sourcing, entity models, tasking graphs.
3. **Fleet telemetry ingestion at swarm scale** — Replicator envisions tens of thousands of small drones at ~10Hz each plus EO/IR/SAR streams. Backhaul is Kafka/Pulsar/NATS JetStream with Flink/Spark processors and Iceberg/Parquet lakes in GovCloud.
4. **Simulation infrastructure** — K8s + Ray + GPU autoscaling + MCAP replay + scenario DSLs + CI-for-autonomy. Shield AI's Aechelon acquisition and Anduril's internal sim are examples.
5. **Autonomy data engines** — Tesla-style trigger/mine/label/retrain/regression pipelines using Temporal, Airflow, or Dagster with DVC or LakeFS dataset versioning.
6. **Mission planning** — graph search, constrained optimization, deconfliction; your math PhD helps more here than backend experience alone.
7. **Edge-to-tactical-to-enterprise tiering** — CRDT-style state sync, store-and-forward, delta-encoded replication across Jetson/Menace-T/tactical cloud/GovCloud.

Peer products worth studying alongside Lattice: **Palantir Gotham/AIP** is the enterprise-tier ontology/data-engineering platform with a stronger ACL and lineage model but less tactical-edge orientation (Java/TypeScript plus the Apache stack underneath, Foundry as a full Spark/Arrow lakehouse). **Shield AI Hivemind** is a pure mission-autonomy stack — AI pilot, SLAM, state estimation, RL-discovered tactics — platform-agnostic by ambition, with a reported **$1.5B Series G at $12.7B valuation in 2026**. It has no data-mesh layer equivalent to Lattice Mesh, which is why the two are complementary and why the in-flight swap demonstration mattered. **L3Harris Amorphous** and **Lockheed's MDCX** are traditional primes' takes on open mission architecture.

## 5. The AI/ML surface, and the narrow slice where a pure-math PhD is a weapon

Computer vision for ATR and tracking is the highest-dollar ML workload in defense today, but it is also the most commoditized — YOLO-family detectors, DETR variants, ByteTrack/BoT-SORT, TensorRT on Jetson with 30-100ms latency budgets. This is a good place to build portfolio, not a place where your PhD differentiates you.

**Reinforcement learning for autonomy is where the math gets serious and defense has produced the clearest public milestones.** DARPA **AlphaDogfight Trials (2019-2020)** had eight teams training deep-RL agents for simulated F-16 dogfights; Heron Systems won and then defeated a USAF Weapons Instructor 5-0 in the human-vs-AI finale (Lockheed's second-place entry used hierarchical RL with maximum-entropy SAC — see `arxiv.org/abs/2105.00990`). **DARPA ACE (Air Combat Evolution)** moved those lessons onto the **X-62A VISTA** modified F-16D at Edwards, with 21 test flights and the first ML-agent-vs-live-manned-F-16 dogfight in September 2023, publicly disclosed April 2024. SECAF Frank Kendall flew in the autonomous F-16 in 2024. Shield AI (post-Heron) was central. The algorithmic toolkit — MDP/POMDP theory, PPO/SAC/TD3, maximum-entropy RL, hierarchical RL, distributional RL, offline RL (CQL/IQL for logged flight data), multi-agent RL (MAPPO, QMIX) for swarms, and game-theoretic equilibria for n-v-m air combat — is native habitat for a math PhD.

**LLMs for operator interfaces** have gone from demo to contract quickly. Palantir AIP for Defense runs RAG over the Gotham/Foundry ontology with human-in-the-loop gates for destructive actions; its ceiling hit **$1.3B in May 2025**, NATO signed on in September 2025 (UK £750M). **Anthropic's Claude Gov** (June 2025) runs in classified AWS enclaves, and CDAO awarded Anthropic a **$200M OTA in July 2025** for frontier capabilities; Claude is reachable from Palantir MSS via FedStart at IL-6. Anduril announced partnerships with OpenAI (2024) and Anthropic for perception and C2 workloads. The engineering challenges — prompt-injection resistance, provenance (Lagrange demonstrated zk-proof integration with the Lattice SDK in October 2025 using DeepProve for per-inference ZK proofs), hallucination containment for kinetic contexts, sensor-grounded agent loops over ontology APIs — are all open.

The narrow areas where a pure-math PhD is differentiating, not interchangeable:

- **Optimal control and estimation theory**: EKF/UKF, particle filters, Rao-Blackwellized PFs, factor-graph smoothing (iSAM2), invariant EKF on Lie groups (Barrau/Bonnabel). The underlying math is probability on manifolds, SO(3)/SE(3), and stochastic calculus.
- **Convex optimization**: MPC, SOCP/QP trajectory optimization, sum-of-squares programming for verification. Stanford ASL (Pavone), MIT LIDS (Tedrake, Slotine), Caltech (Doyle, Chung).
- **Differential geometry and topology in SLAM**: SE(3) factor graphs, invariant filtering, persistent homology for topological mapping. The best SLAM papers are 80% differential geometry.
- **Information theory in sensor fusion**: Fisher-information sensor placement, mutual-information-maximizing active perception, Cramér-Rao bounds.
- **Game theory for adversarial planning**: differential games (Isaacs, Hamilton-Jacobi-Isaacs reachability), level-k and cognitive-hierarchy models, Stackelberg equilibria in pursuer-evader and RoE-constrained combat, no-regret learning for repeated-game strategies. Directly relevant to ACE-class work.
- **Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman PDEs** for verified reachability envelopes around learned controllers — airworthiness-grade safety cases for ML-in-the-loop control. Ian Mitchell's toolboxes and Claire Tomlin's Berkeley Hybrid Systems Lab are the canonical groups.
- **Rare-event analysis and formal verification** — importance sampling and splitting for safety cases, Koopman-operator methods for verification of learned controllers.

Academic labs to track: **UPenn GRASP** (Kumar, Pappas, Daniilidis), **MIT CSAIL/LIDS** (Tedrake, Roy, How — Jonathan How's ACL is directly defense-aligned), **Stanford ASL and SISL** (Pavone, Kochenderfer), **Caltech AMBER/CAST** (Ames, Chung, Burdick), **Georgia Tech DCSL** (Tsiotras), **CMU RI and AirLab** (Scherer), **ETH RPG and ASL** (Scaramuzza, Siegwart, Hutter), **Oxford ORI**. In Korea: **H. Jin Kim's SNU Aerial Robotics Lab, Hyunchul Shim's KAIST Unmanned Systems Research Group, and Frank Park's SNU Robotics Lab** — the last two are your obvious networking targets, and Park is literally a co-author of *Modern Robotics*.

## 7. A 12-month learning plan calibrated for ~10 hours per week

The opinionated claim first: **if you only do three things, do (a) Beard & McLain's *Small Unmanned Aircraft* with the MATLAB/Python chapter-by-chapter project, (b) Lynch & Park's *Modern Robotics* Coursera specialization — Frank Park is at SNU, the Korean translation is by Acorn Publishing, and his SNU lectures are available in Korean if you prefer dense derivations in your native language — and (c) a PX4 SITL + ROS 2 Jazzy + Gazebo Harmonic stack with a companion-computer perception loop running on your own Pixhawk 6X and Jetson Orin Nano.** Everything else is scaffolding.

**Tier-1 textbooks** (work through, not just reference): **Beard & McLain, *Small Unmanned Aircraft: Theory and Practice*** (Princeton, 2012; working second-edition PDF and the 14-chapter Python simulation project at `github.com/byu-magicc/mavsim_public` — the book's defining feature is that each chapter adds a layer to a fixed-wing MAV sim, which is perfect for a software engineer). **Lynch & Park, *Modern Robotics*** (Cambridge, 2017; free preprint at `hades.mech.northwestern.edu`; screw-theory / product-of-exponentials formulation that pays off throughout robotics). **Thrun/Burgard/Fox, *Probabilistic Robotics*** (MIT Press, 2005; chapters 2-9 and 13-15, skip later). **Barfoot, *State Estimation for Robotics*, 2nd ed.** (Cambridge, 2024; free PDF at `asrl.utias.utoronto.ca/~tdb/` — the right modern pairing to Thrun for SE(3), batch/sliding-window, and factor graphs). **Szeliski, *Computer Vision*, 2nd ed.** (Springer, 2022; free at `szeliski.org/Book/`).

**Tier-2 references**: **Åström & Murray, *Feedback Systems*, 2nd ed.** (Princeton, 2021; free at `fbswiki.org`). **Simon, *Optimal State Estimation*** for Kalman filtering. **Bar-Shalom/Li/Kirubarajan, *Estimation with Applications to Tracking and Navigation*** — the defense-industry tracking bible (IMM, JPDA, multi-target). **Liberzon, *Calculus of Variations and Optimal Control Theory***, which your pure-math half will find more satisfying than Kirk. **Bertsekas, *Dynamic Programming and Optimal Control*, Vol. I, 4th ed.** for the MDP foundations under RL.

**Tier-3 defense strategic context**: **Chris Brose, *The Kill Chain*** (Hachette, 2020 — read first; it is the Anduril cultural canon). **Raj Shah & Christopher Kirchhoff, *Unit X*** (Scribner, 2024 — inside account of DIU and the actual programs you'll see in job descriptions). **Chris Miller, *Chip War*** for supply chain. KIDA (Korea Institute for Defense Analyses) annual reports at `kida.re.kr` for Korean-specific context.

**Online courses (verified 2026)** — start with the **Modern Robotics Specialization** (Coursera, Kevin Lynch, Northwestern; mirrors the Lynch & Park textbook and uses free CoppeliaSim), add **Aerial Robotics** (Coursera, Vijay Kumar, UPenn — four weeks, canonical quadrotor dynamics/control), and pair with the **Self-Driving Cars Specialization** (Coursera, Waslander & Kelly, UToronto/Waterloo — best practical treatment of perception and sensor fusion you can get online, and transfers cleanly to drones). Then go deep with **MIT 6.832 Underactuated Robotics** (Tedrake, free on OCW/YouTube with the online textbook at `underactuated.mit.edu` and the Drake toolbox — the single best graduate-level treatment of drone control) and **MIT 16.485 Visual Navigation for Autonomous Vehicles** (Luca Carlone, free — SE(3), factor graphs, VIO). For SLAM, **Cyrill Stachniss's 2020-2022 YouTube playlists** are the best free instructor in the world; his older 2013 playlist is superseded. **Steve Brunton's YouTube** (UW) is the best intuition-first controls teacher. For ML, **Karpathy's "Neural Networks: Zero to Hero"** is mandatory if you want to actually understand modern perception nets. **Skip Udacity's Flying Car Nanodegree** — still listed but unmaintained since ~2019, hardware target (Intel Aero) discontinued. Skip the Udacity Self-Driving Car Nanodegree too — UToronto reconstructs 90% of it free.

## 8. An honest assessment

**This is a compelling area to commit to long-term if three things are true for you**: you can make peace with working on systems whose end-state is kinetic effect on a human target; you are willing to engage with the Korean ecosystem as your primary career ground rather than waiting for a US firm to call; and you find the math-and-backend-systems nexus (factor graphs, RL theory, CQRS data meshes, edge-to-cloud tiering) genuinely interesting rather than merely interesting-adjacent. If all three are true, the next five years are unusually accommodating: the field is under-supplied with senior engineers who combine distributed-systems muscle with mathematical fluency, the Korean government has put real money behind 100 defense startups by 2030, and the algorithmic core of the field is having its "Waymo → foundation-driver" moment simultaneously across autonomy, simulation, and C2 — which means whatever you learn in the next 12 months will compound unusually fast.

**The highest-leverage entry points for your specific profile, ranked**, are: **(1) Nearthlab** — Seoul-based, pre-IPO, real defense revenue, real AI stack, Korean-speed but English-friendly, product surface (computer vision + flight autonomy + mesh swarm) matches your skill set almost exactly. **(2) Bone AI** — Seoul + Palo Alto, repeat founder, Third Prime + Kolon backed, explicit "Korean Anduril" thesis, highest upside with highest execution risk. **(3) LIG Nex1 AI / Unmanned Systems division or Hanwha Systems AI division** — direct involvement in MUCP, AI Swarm Drone, K-AILOT-adjacent work, and the emerging Krafton-Hanwha physical-AI joint venture. Most stability, slowest velocity. **(4) Korean Air Aerospace Division (Anduril JV)** — the backdoor into Anduril tech without ITAR blocking, especially if "Arsenal South Korea" materializes. **(5) UVify** — newly flush with KRW 60B and moving into defense physical-AI. **(6) Stradvision or an equivalent AD perception company** as a stepping-stone if you want to build perception-in-production scar tissue before moving to defense.

**Do not expect direct hiring by Anduril Korea, Shield AI, or Skydio for core engineering roles in Seoul near-term** — Anduril Korea is deliberately a customer-engineering / TPM office, and ITAR closes everything else unless you pursue the 5-10-year naturalization path. **Get a 비밀취급인가 clearance early**; it transfers between Korean defense employers and accelerates future moves.

**The ethical and strategic downsides deserve flat honesty.** You are considering work whose end state is systems that kill people. The specific strategic context matters: the Korean case for indigenous military drone capability is one of the cleaner ones morally — a democratic state facing a nuclear-armed adversary 50 km north, a conscription crisis driven by demographic collapse, and a defense industry that needs to not be the thing that fails when it matters. Shield AI's and Anduril's US case is debatable; the Korean case is, in my view, defensible on deterrence grounds. But the work itself forces you to decide how you feel about autonomy-on-the-move (weapons that select and engage targets without a human in the loop at the moment of release), about dual-use collateral (the same ATR model that identifies enemy armor can misidentify a civilian truck), and about the fact that your employer's incentive is to ship, iterate, and win contracts faster than the adversary. Chris Brose and Trae Stephens argue deterrence saves lives on net; the counter-argument from groups like the Future of Life Institute and ICRAC is that autonomous lethality lowers the threshold for war. Both positions are serious. Engage with them directly before committing — read Stephens's "The Reluctant Warrior," Paul Scharre's *Army of None*, and the ICRAC position papers, and make your own call.

**How the field will evolve over 5-10 years**: expect further compression of the gap between "RL policy in simulation" and "autonomy that flies a real CCA in combat" — ACE, AlphaDogfight, and the Hivemind-Lattice February 2026 in-flight swap are dress rehearsals, and the full production version will arrive around the 2028-2030 CCA Increment 2 / 3 window. Expect **foundation-model-driven autonomy** (Shield AI's Hivemind Foundation Model, Anduril's direction post-Aechelon-equivalent internal sim, Waymo's EMMA-style end-to-end drivers) to converge across AV and defense as the underlying transformer architectures become commodity. Expect C2 fabrics to standardize around a small number of schemas (Lattice's Entities/Tasks/Objects, Palantir's ontology, Maven's model hub) as the DoD pushes Open DAGIR and CDAO continues to fund mesh infrastructure. Expect **counter-UAS and directed energy** to become the next hot subsystem as Ukraine's Drone Line catalyzes cheap attritable offense, forcing equally cheap defense. Expect the Korean industry to consolidate into roughly three tiers — chaebol primes (LIG Nex1, Hanwha, KAI, KAL) as integrators, a half-dozen serious startups (Nearthlab, Bone AI, UVify, and two or three not yet visible) as autonomy/perception innovators, and a long tail of sensor/component plays — and expect at least one of those startups to reach unicorn status by 2029. Expect ITAR/EAR to loosen modestly for AUKUS and Korea-Japan-Australia partners under the pressure of a China contingency, which may open more Anduril-Korea engineering roles than exist today.

**The one concrete action to take in the next two weeks**: buy the Crazyflie, clone `mavsim_public`, and start Beard & McLain chapter 1. Everything else in this report is downstream of whether you actually enjoy the first 40 hours of doing this work. If by month two you are energized by watching your own EKF innovations converge in PX4 logs, commit further. If you're not, the honest signal is to stay in backend engineering with an AI/ML slant and revisit this in two years when the Korean ecosystem has matured further. Your math PhD and your Kafka-scale backend chops will still be valuable then — possibly more so.
