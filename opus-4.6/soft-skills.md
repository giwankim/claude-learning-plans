# The software engineer's guide to building soft skills

**The most impactful career investments for engineers aren't technical — they're human.** The resources below represent the most highly recommended books, courses, podcasts, and communities for software engineers who want to communicate more effectively, influence leadership, and lead engineering teams. These were curated from recommendations across Hacker News, r/ExperiencedDevs, prominent engineering blogs, and endorsements by leaders at companies like Google, Stripe, Uber, and Shopify. The list prioritizes resources built specifically for engineers over generic business fare, though it includes widely respected general titles that have become staples in tech circles.

---

## Communication: the skill that compounds fastest

Strong communication separates senior engineers from staff engineers and beyond. The challenge is unique in tech — you must translate complex systems into clear narratives for audiences ranging from fellow engineers to executive stakeholders who've never written a line of code.

### Books for written and verbal communication

**On Writing Well** by William Zinsser is the single most recommended writing book across engineering communities. It teaches clarity, simplicity, and the ruthless elimination of clutter — skills that directly improve design docs, RFCs, and Slack messages alike. Paul Graham and Ryan Peterman (Instagram) both endorse it as essential for engineers. For documentation specifically, **Docs for Developers** by Jared Bhatti et al. (authored by tech writers from Google, Stripe, and the Linux Foundation) treats documentation as an engineering practice, covering the full lifecycle from READMEs to API docs to tutorials.

**The Pyramid Principle** by Barbara Minto teaches top-down, conclusion-first structuring — the gold standard for technical proposals and executive summaries. Engineers who adopt this framework for design docs and project pitches see immediate results. For interpersonal communication, **Crucial Conversations** by Kerry Patterson et al. provides frameworks for high-stakes discussions where emotions and opinions collide, directly applicable to code review disagreements, timeline pushbacks, and architecture debates.

For presentations, two books stand out. **Talk Like TED** by Carmine Gallo distills the principles behind the most successful TED talks into actionable techniques — particularly the Rule of Three for structuring technical presentations. **Made to Stick** by Chip and Dan Heath explains why some ideas are memorable using the SUCCESs framework (Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories), which is invaluable when pitching technical ideas to non-technical stakeholders.

**Communication for Engineers** by Chris Laffra (foreword by Google SVP Urs Hölzle) is the most directly targeted book in this space, written by an engineer who worked at IBM, Google, and Uber. It covers everything from code-reviews-as-communication to stakeholder meetings, addressing the specific challenges engineers face daily.

### Courses and workshops worth your time

**Google's Technical Writing Courses** (free at developers.google.com/tech-writing) are the clear winner for technical writing. Developed internally at Google and taken by thousands of their engineers, they cover clarity, structure, active voice, and document organization in roughly 5–6 hours of self-study, with optional live sessions offered periodically.

The **Communication Skills for Engineers Specialization** from Rice University on Coursera is the most comprehensive engineer-specific communication program on any major platform. Its three courses span oral communication, interpersonal skills, and professional writing, taught by the Rice Center for Engineering Leadership. For ongoing practice, **Toastmasters International** remains unmatched — many companies including Google, Microsoft, and Amazon run internal clubs, and the structured feedback model resonates with engineers' analytical mindsets at roughly $50 per six months.

## Managing up without managing people

Managing up — influencing leadership, setting expectations, and advocating for technical decisions — is perhaps the least taught but most career-defining soft skill for engineers. It determines whether your best ideas get resources and whether your team's work gets recognized.

### The essential reading list

**The Staff Engineer's Path** by Tanya Reilly (O'Reilly, 2022) and **Staff Engineer** by Will Larson (2021) are the two foundational texts. Reilly's book covers the three pillars of staff-level work — big-picture thinking, cross-team execution, and leading without authority — with extensive practical guidance on communicating with leadership. Larson's book, built on **12+ interviews with Staff+ engineers** at Stripe, Uber, and others, includes the crucial chapter "Stay Aligned with Authority" on partnering with your management chain. Together, they map the terrain of influence as a senior IC.

For the mechanics of persuasion, **Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion** by Robert Cialdini provides a scientific framework for understanding why people say yes, built on seven universal principles backed by 35 years of research. **Influence Without Authority** by Cohen and Bradford introduces the Exchange Model — getting cooperation from people you don't control by understanding what they value — which directly addresses the core challenge engineers face when advocating for technical investments.

The most recent and practically targeted book is **Managing Up** by Melody Wilding (2025), which distills the skill into 10 key conversations with word-for-word scripts and templates. For engineers who prefer structured frameworks (and most do), this is the most actionable resource available. Pair it with **The Manager's Path** by Camille Fournier — even if you're not a manager, understanding how managers think is the most effective way to communicate with them.

**How to Win Friends and Influence People** by Dale Carnegie remains the most-mentioned soft skills book across all of Reddit for good reason. Carnegie himself observed that "the highest-paid personnel in engineering are frequently not those who know the most about engineering." The interpersonal dynamics it teaches — genuine interest, avoiding criticism, focusing on others' needs — directly determine promotions and influence in engineering organizations.

### Courses and frameworks

Arizona State University offers a dedicated **Managing Up** course on Coursera covering rapport-building, upward feedback, and credibility development. LinkedIn Learning provides several focused options, including **Managing Up, Down, and Across the Organization** (a concise 40-minute intermediate course on influence without authority) and a companion course by Mary Abbajay that provides taxonomies of boss types with specific strategies for each.

For a framework-driven approach, Luca Rossi's article **"Frameworks for Managing Up as a Software Engineer"** on High Growth Engineer (from the author of Refactoring.fm, read by 140,000+ engineers) provides models specifically designed for how engineers think about relationships with their managers.

## From IC to engineering leader: managing other engineers

The transition from individual contributor to tech lead or engineering manager is one of the most difficult career shifts in software. Your value no longer comes from code you write but from the output of the people you lead — a fundamentally different skill set that requires deliberate development.

### Tier 1 books every new engineering leader needs

**The Manager's Path** by Camille Fournier is universally cited as the single most important book for the transition. It maps each career stage — from mentoring to tech lead to managing a team to managing managers — with practical guidance at each level. Goodreads rating of **4.25 across 10,000+ reviews**, and virtually every "best EM books" list puts it at the top.

**Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager** by James Stanier is the most tactical companion. Written by a CS PhD who made the transition himself, it covers the nitty-gritty: organizing your time, running 1:1s, giving performance reviews, delegation, hiring, and firing. Gergely Orosz called it "the book I'll be recommending to new managers transitioning into the role."

**Resilient Management** by Lara Hogan (former VP Engineering at Kickstarter) packs extraordinary value into roughly 100 pages. Built around the **BICEPS framework** for understanding teammates' core needs and Tuckman's stages of group development, it's the fastest path to becoming effective at 1:1s, feedback, and building team resilience.

**An Elegant Puzzle** by Will Larson treats engineering management as a systems problem — team sizing, technical debt management, succession planning, and organizational design — drawn from experience at Uber, Stripe, and Calm. For engineers who think in systems, this book makes the ambiguous world of management feel structured and solvable.

### Books for deepening your practice

**High Output Management** by Andy Grove remains the foundational management text in Silicon Valley despite being from 1983. Its concepts of output-based thinking, task-relevant maturity, and meeting design are directly applicable to engineering teams. Ben Horowitz wrote the foreword for the reissue. **Radical Candor** by Kim Scott provides the most widely adopted feedback framework in tech — the "Care Personally / Challenge Directly" matrix — essential for the feedback-giving that new managers find most daunting.

**Engineering Management for the Rest of Us** by Sarah Drasner (VP Developer Experience at Google, formerly Netlify) is written specifically for engineers who never imagined themselves as managers. **The Making of a Manager** by Julie Zhuo, who became a manager at Facebook at 25, offers a relatable first-person account of early leadership uncertainty. And **Turn the Ship Around!** by L. David Marquet, one of the most recommended leadership books in tech circles, demonstrates how to transform command-and-control environments into leader-leader models where autonomous engineering teams thrive.

For team organization at scale, **Team Topologies** by Skelton and Pais provides frameworks for structuring engineering teams around flow and interaction modes. **Accelerate** by Forsgren, Humble, and Kim gives data-driven frameworks (the DORA metrics) for measuring and improving team performance based on research across 2,000+ companies.

### Courses and programs for new managers

Cohort-based courses on **Maven** have emerged as the leading platform for engineering management training. Notable options include **Senior Engineer to Lead** by Gregor Ojstersek (focused on the IC-to-lead transition across tech lead, staff, and EM tracks), **Engineering Leadership in the AI Era** by Kelly Vaughn (Senior EM at Zapier, covering communication, feedback, and mentoring frameworks), and the **Pragmatic Engineering Manager** course by Ananth Ramachandran (limited to 15 participants per cohort for personalized feedback).

On LinkedIn Learning, **Moving from Developer to Engineering Manager** by Jessica Rose specifically targets the transition, covering the shift from hands-on coding to coaching and advocacy. For mentorship rather than courses, **Plato** (platohq.com, now part of Coda) pairs new engineering managers with experienced mentors from Google, Netflix, Spotify, and others — the premier mentorship platform in this space, used by companies like Airbnb and Stripe.

## Podcasts, newsletters, and communities that keep you growing

Leadership development isn't a one-time investment. The best engineering leaders maintain ongoing learning habits through curated content and peer communities.

### Podcasts for your commute

- **The Pragmatic Engineer Podcast** (Gergely Orosz) — deep dives with experienced engineers on building software, leading teams, and navigating career growth at Big Tech and high-growth startups. Gergely's former EM experience at Uber brings unusual depth to organizational and political dynamics.
- **Supermanagers** (Aydin Mirzaee, Fellow.app) — interviews with executives from Shopify, Dropbox, and others focused squarely on the craft of management: 1:1s, feedback, culture, and scaling teams. Features guests like Lara Hogan.
- **The Engineering Leadership Podcast** (Patrick Gallagher & Jerry Li) — weekly interviews with engineering leaders from OpenAI, Shopify, and PayPal covering scaling teams, managing crises, and building engineering culture.
- **Developer Tea** (Jonathan Cutrell) — short episodes under 15 minutes on career growth, communication, and mindset shifts. Perfect for building a daily learning habit with minimal time commitment.
- **Effective Engineering Manager** (Slava Imeshev & Adam Axelrod) — rated **4.8/5 on Apple Podcasts**, provides proven solutions for delegation, cross-functional collaboration, and people management.

### Newsletters worth subscribing to

**The Pragmatic Engineer** by Gergely Orosz is the gold standard — **500,000+ subscribers** making it the #1 technology newsletter on Substack, with deep dives into engineering management, compensation, hiring, and organizational dynamics. **Software Lead Weekly** by Oren Ellenbogen (VP Engineering at Forter) curates the best stories on leadership, soft skills, and engineering culture each week to roughly 30,000 subscribers and is called "the most consistently excellent mailing list" by tech leads.

**Engineering Leadership** by Gregor Ojstersek provides weekly actionable guidance with real-world examples and full roadmaps for breaking into engineering leadership. **Level Up** by Pat Kua draws on 20+ years of tech leadership experience. **The Engineering Manager** by James Stanier (Director of Engineering at Shopify) offers tools and tips grounded in daily management reality. And **Will Larson's blog** at lethain.com provides some of the most referenced thinking in the field on engineering strategy, team design, and leadership philosophy.

### Communities where engineering leaders gather

**Rands Leadership Slack** (30,000+ members, founded by Michael Lopp of Apple) is the largest and most respected community for engineering leaders, operating under Chatham House Rule for candid discussions. **Engineering Managers Slack** is more focused on day-to-day EM challenges and is rated the best community specifically for first-time engineering managers. **LeadDev** combines conferences, articles, and a 7,000-member Slack community featuring contributors like Charity Majors and Michael Lopp. The **ELC (Engineering Leadership Community)** at sfelc.com offers Slack, conferences, and peer group programs for VP/Director-level leaders.

## How to build your learning path

The resources above are most effective when approached deliberately rather than consumed all at once. For engineers at different stages, here's where to start:

| Your current stage | Start with these | Then add |
|---|---|---|
| Mid-level IC wanting to communicate better | *On Writing Well*, Google Technical Writing courses, *Crucial Conversations* | *The Pyramid Principle*, Toastmasters |
| Senior IC building influence | *The Staff Engineer's Path*, *Influence Without Authority*, *Managing Up* (Wilding) | *Staff Engineer* (Larson), High Growth Engineer newsletter |
| New tech lead or EM | *The Manager's Path*, *Resilient Management*, Engineering Managers Slack | *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, *Radical Candor*, Supermanagers podcast |
| Experienced EM scaling up | *An Elegant Puzzle*, *High Output Management*, *Team Topologies* | Rands Leadership Slack, The Pragmatic Engineer newsletter, Maven cohort courses |

The common thread across every recommendation is that soft skills for engineers aren't soft at all — they're precise, learnable disciplines with frameworks, mental models, and deliberate practice. The engineers who invest in these resources consistently report that communication and leadership skills, not technical depth alone, were what unlocked the next stage of their careers.
